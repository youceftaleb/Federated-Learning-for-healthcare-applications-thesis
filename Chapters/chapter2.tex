\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:ch2-intro}
% ============================================================

Modern healthcare generates massive quantities of sensitive clinical
data -- radiological images, genomic profiles, electronic health records,
and physiological time series -- distributed across hospitals, research
centres, and diagnostic laboratories that are separated by
institutional, legal, and geographical boundaries.
Chapter~1 established that these institutions operate as data silos:
each custodian holds locally valuable patient data that it cannot, in
practice, freely share with external parties.
The regulatory frameworks that govern this reality -- the General Data
Protection Regulation (GDPR) in the European Union, the Health
Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) in the United
States, and, in the Algerian context directly applicable to this work,
Law~18-07 of June~10,~2018 relative to the protection of
natural persons in the processing of personal data, which
establishes equivalent protections under the supervision
of the Autorité Nationale de Protection des Données à
Caractère Personnel (ANPDP)\footnote{Journal Officiel de la République
Algérienne, No.~34, June~10,~2018. Law 18-07 relative to the protection
of natural persons in the processing of personal data.} -- impose strict
conditions on any cross-institutional transfer of patient data, conditions
that a conventional centralised machine learning pipeline cannot satisfy
without substantial legal and technical overhead
\cite{gdpr2016regulation,hipaa1996health}.
In Algeria, the same obligations apply under
Law~18-07, which governs any processing of health data
by Algerian institutions regardless of whether the data
are transferred abroad.

Yet the imperative for collaborative learning in clinical AI is equally
compelling.
Deep learning models for medical image analysis are notoriously
data-hungry; training a convolutional network to classify breast MRI
molecular subtypes from a single institution's cohort risks producing a
model that is overfit to that institution's scanner hardware, patient
demographics, and acquisition protocol, and that fails to generalise to
other sites \cite{rieke2020future}.
Meaningful generalisation across clinical environments -- a prerequisite
for safe deployment -- requires exposure to the diversity that is only
available when multiple institutions contribute to the learning process.

Federated learning (FL) resolves this tension by inverting the
conventional data-movement assumption: instead of bringing patient data
to the model, the model is brought to the data.
Each participating institution trains a local copy of the model on its
own private data and transmits only model parameters -- gradient updates
or weight vectors -- to a central coordination server that aggregates them
into an improved global model.
Raw patient data never leave the custodian site, and the progressively
aggregated model captures knowledge distributed across all participating
institutions \cite{mcmahan2017communication,kairouz2021advances}.

This chapter provides a structured survey of federated learning as it
applies to healthcare, with particular attention to the aspects
relevant to the breast MRI molecular subtype classification task
studied in this thesis.
Section~\ref{sec:centralised-to-fl} traces the conceptual and
technical path from centralised to distributed learning and formally
defines federated learning.
Section~\ref{sec:fl-architecture} describes the canonical FL
client--server architecture and the communication protocol that
structures training rounds.
Section~\ref{sec:aggregation} surveys the principal aggregation
strategies and their robustness to non-identically distributed (non-IID)
data across clients.
Sections~\ref{sec:communication}--\ref{sec:frameworks} address
communication efficiency, privacy and security mechanisms, convergence
challenges, and the major open-source FL frameworks.
Section~\ref{sec:related-work} reviews the most relevant related work
in medical imaging FL, and Section~\ref{sec:ch2-conclusion} concludes
by positioning the present contribution within the state of the art.

\section{From Centralised to Distributed Learning}
\label{sec:centralised-to-fl}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Limitations of Centralised Learning in Healthcare}
\label{sec:centralised-limits}

The dominant paradigm in machine learning assumes that training data
can be assembled on a single computing infrastructure: all images,
labels, and metadata are collected into a shared repository from which
a model is optimised over the entire dataset in one place.
While this paradigm has produced outstanding results in computer vision
and natural language processing using large internet-sourced corpora,
its direct application to clinical medical imaging raises a set of
fundamental obstacles that are qualitatively distinct from those
encountered in industrial or consumer settings.

\paragraph{Privacy and data-sovereignty constraints.}
Patient health data is one of the most sensitive categories of personal
information recognised by law.
The GDPR classifies health data as a \emph{special category} of
personal data whose processing requires an explicit legal basis and, in
the absence of a recognised public-interest justification, the freely
given informed consent of the data subject \cite{gdpr2016regulation}.
HIPAA similarly mandates that covered entities implement technical,
physical, and administrative safeguards against unauthorised disclosure
of protected health information (PHI) \cite{hipaa1996health}.
Transmitting raw breast MRI volumes to an external data centre
constitutes a cross-institutional transfer of PHI; each volume may be
re-identified through its anatomical characteristics, and the transfer
itself may trigger obligations under both frameworks.
In practice, hospital data protection officers and ethics committees
are frequently unwilling or legally unable to approve such transfers
without case-by-case ethical approval -- a process whose time and
administrative cost renders systematic centralisation infeasible for
multi-site studies.

\paragraph{Data ownership and institutional interests.}
Beyond the regulatory dimension, institutional data ownership creates
strategic tensions.
A hospital's radiological archive represents years of clinical
investment and constitutes a competitive asset for academic medical
centres engaged in research productivity metrics.
Participating institutions may be willing to contribute to collaborative
research while remaining unwilling to surrender physical custody of
their imaging collections to a third-party data aggregator.
Centralised data pooling, by design, requires that contributing
institutions relinquish effective control over how their data are
stored, accessed, and potentially re-used by the operator of the
central repository \cite{rieke2020future}.
This tension has been a practical barrier to the construction of
large, multi-centre imaging databases even where formal data-sharing
agreements exist in principle.

\paragraph{Transfer cost and technical scalability.}
Breast MRI volumes acquired with dynamic contrast-enhanced (DCE-MRI)
protocols are large.
A single patient examination -- comprising multiple dynamic phases and
high-resolution three-dimensional sequences -- can occupy several
gigabytes of storage.
When a dataset spans hundreds of patients across multiple geographically
distributed institutions, the aggregate data-transfer burden in terms
of bandwidth, storage provisioning, and network latency is
substantial, particularly for clinical sites in under-resourced
network environments.
This logistical barrier can render centralised data pooling impractical
independently of any regulatory consideration.

\paragraph{Single-site bias and generalisation failure.}
Even when centralisation is technically and legally feasible, a model
trained exclusively on data from a single institution carries the risk
of overfitting to institution-specific covariates: the scanner
manufacturer and field strength, the surface coil configuration, the
contrast-agent protocol, and the specific patient demographic served by
that hospital.
In breast MRI molecular subtype classification, where the relative
prevalence of Luminal~A, Luminal~B, HER2-enriched, and Triple-Negative
subtypes varies with ethnicity, age, and clinical screening policy,
a single-site model may achieve high internal validation accuracy while
failing on data from a second site with a different subtype distribution
and acquisition parameters \cite{rieke2020future,sheller2020federated}.
This limitation is clinically consequential: a deployed model that does
not generalise across sites introduces systematic diagnostic errors in
any population it has not seen during training.

\subsection{Distributed Learning Paradigms}
\label{sec:distributed-paradigms}

The limitations of centralised learning have motivated a spectrum of
distributed training approaches that differ in where data reside, how
computation is partitioned, and the degree of trust placed in the
coordinating infrastructure.
Understanding where federated learning sits within this spectrum
clarifies both its distinctive properties and the assumptions it makes.

\paragraph{Classical data-parallel distributed training.}
The most straightforward departure from single-machine training is
data-parallel processing in a parameter-server architecture
\cite{dean2012large}.
A central server maintains the canonical copy of model parameters;
a pool of worker machines each receive a disjoint shard of a centrally
stored dataset, compute gradients over that shard, and return the
gradients to the server for synchronous or asynchronous aggregation.
This architecture scales computation across many machines and has
become standard practice in industry for training large-scale models.
Crucially, however, the data themselves remain co-located in a shared
data centre under the exclusive control of a single operator.
Data-parallel distributed training therefore does not address the
privacy, sovereignty, or data-transfer cost concerns identified in the
previous section; it is an engineering technique for accelerating
computation over a centralised dataset, not a solution to the
multi-institutional data-governance problem.

\paragraph{Federated learning as a privacy-aware cross-silo paradigm.}
Federated learning introduces a qualitatively different constraint:
the training data are not co-located and, by design or by legal
requirement, cannot be moved to a central facility.
Each data silo -- referred to in the FL literature as a
\emph{client} -- retains its data locally and participates in the
training process by computing model updates on its own private dataset
and transmitting only those updates, never the raw data, to a
coordinating server \cite{mcmahan2017communication}.
The server aggregates parameter updates from participating clients and
broadcasts an improved global model for the next training round.
Raw patient records never traverse the network, and the communication
channel carries only floating-point model weights whose privacy
properties can be further reinforced with cryptographic or
noise-injection techniques (discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:privacy-security}).

FL is commonly classified along two orthogonal axes that determine the
applicable algorithms and privacy assumptions.
The first axis distinguishes \emph{horizontal} from \emph{vertical}
federation.
In \emph{horizontal} FL, all clients share the same feature space -- all
institutions acquire the same imaging modality and predict the same
outcome -- but hold non-overlapping patient populations.
This is the setting relevant to the present thesis: each of the three
simulated hospital clients holds a distinct cohort of breast MRI
patients with the same four molecular subtype labels.
In \emph{vertical} FL, clients hold overlapping patient identifiers
but complementary feature spaces -- for example, one institution holds
MRI images and another holds genomic profiles for the same patients.
Vertical federation requires patient-identifier alignment across sites,
which introduces additional privacy challenges and is a more complex
engineering problem \cite{yang2019federated,kairouz2021advances}.

The second axis distinguishes \emph{cross-device} from
\emph{cross-silo} federation.
Cross-device FL involves a very large number of lightweight,
intermittently available clients -- potentially millions of smartphones
or Internet-of-Things sensors -- communicating over unreliable
low-bandwidth links with highly heterogeneous hardware capabilities.
Cross-silo FL, by contrast, involves a small to moderate number of
institutional clients -- a consortium of hospitals or research
centres -- that are persistently available, possess substantial compute
resources, and are typically bound by formal data-sharing agreements
even if raw data transfer is prohibited.
The breast MRI classification scenario studied in this work corresponds
to \emph{cross-silo horizontal} FL: three simulated hospital clients,
each holding a non-IID partition of the 737-patient DCE-MRI dataset,
trained using the Flower framework with PyTorch as the local compute
backend.

the main FL paradigms.

\subsection{Definition and Origins of Federated Learning}
\label{sec:fl-definition}

Federated learning was formally introduced by McMahan \emph{et al.}\
in 2017 in the context of training a next-word-prediction language
model on data distributed across millions of mobile devices
\cite{mcmahan2017communication}.
The authors identified a class of learning problems characterised by
three properties: the training data are generated and stored on devices
that are not under the operator's direct control; those data contain
sensitive user information that makes centralised aggregation
undesirable; and the dataset is massively distributed, with each
device holding a relatively small and non-identically distributed
portion of the total information.
The proposed solution -- \emph{Federated Averaging} (FedAvg) -- has since
become the canonical baseline algorithm for the field and is described
in detail in Section~\ref{sec:fedavg}.

Formally, the federated learning optimisation problem is stated as
follows.
Let $K$ denote the number of participating clients, let
$\mathcal{D}_k$ denote the private local dataset of client $k$,
with $n_k = |\mathcal{D}_k|$ the number of local samples and
$n = \sum_{k=1}^{K} n_k$ the total sample count across all clients.
The goal is to find model parameters $\mathbf{w}^{\ast}$ that minimise
the globally weighted empirical risk:

\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{w}^{\ast}
  \;=\;
  \arg\min_{\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^d}
  \sum_{k=1}^{K} \frac{n_k}{n}\, F_k(\mathbf{w}),
  \label{eq:fl-objective}
\end{equation}

where the local objective $F_k$ of client $k$ is the empirical risk
over its private data:

\begin{equation}
  F_k(\mathbf{w})
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{n_k}
  \sum_{(\mathbf{x},\, y)\,\in\,\mathcal{D}_k}
  \ell\bigl(f(\mathbf{x};\mathbf{w}),\; y\bigr),
  \label{eq:local-risk}
\end{equation}

with $f(\cdot\,;\mathbf{w})$ the model function parameterised by
$\mathbf{w}$, and $\ell$ the per-sample loss function (e.g.\
cross-entropy for multi-class classification).
The key observation is that the objective in
\eqref{eq:fl-objective} is \emph{mathematically equivalent} to
standard empirical risk minimisation over the pooled dataset
$\bigcup_k \mathcal{D}_k$, yet it can be minimised
\emph{without ever pooling the data} \cite{kairouz2021advances}.

Training proceeds in discrete \emph{communication rounds}.
At round $t$, the server selects a subset of clients
$\mathcal{S}^{(t)} \subseteq \{1,\ldots,K\}$ and broadcasts the
current global parameters $\mathbf{w}^{(t)}$.
Each selected client $k$ initialises its local model with
$\mathbf{w}^{(t)}$ and performs $E$ steps of mini-batch stochastic
gradient descent on $\mathcal{D}_k$, obtaining a locally updated
parameter vector $\mathbf{w}_k^{(t+1)}$.
The server then aggregates the client updates into a new global
model using a weighted average:

\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{w}^{(t+1)}
  \;=\;
  \sum_{k\,\in\,\mathcal{S}^{(t)}}
  \frac{n_k}{\displaystyle\sum_{j\,\in\,\mathcal{S}^{(t)}} n_j}\;
  \mathbf{w}_k^{(t+1)}.
  \label{eq:fedavg-aggregation}
\end{equation}

Equation~\eqref{eq:fedavg-aggregation} is the aggregation step of
FedAvg; the local training step and its interaction with data
heterogeneity are analysed in detail in Section~\ref{sec:aggregation}.

The transition from the original mobile cross-device setting to
cross-silo healthcare applications was both rapid and well-motivated.
Pioneering empirical work by Sheller \emph{et al.}\ demonstrated
that a FL model trained across multiple institutions, without any
data sharing, could achieve performance competitive with a centrally
trained baseline on a brain tumour segmentation task -- a result
attributable in part to the regularising effect of training on
heterogeneous clinical distributions \cite{sheller2020federated}.
Subsequent comprehensive surveys have catalogued FL applications
spanning radiology, pathology, genomics, and electronic health records
\cite{rieke2020future,nguyen2022federated,kaissis2020secure},
establishing federated learning as the dominant collaborative learning
paradigm endorsed by the medical AI research community when privacy
constraints prohibit raw data sharing.

\section{Federated Learning Architecture}
\label{sec:fl-architecture}
% ============================================================

The practical realisation of the federated optimisation objective
defined in Section~\ref{sec:fl-definition} requires a concrete system
architecture that coordinates communication between geographically
distributed participants, manages model state across training rounds,
and enforces the data-locality constraint that distinguishes federated
from centralised learning.
This section describes the canonical two-tier client--server
architecture that underlies most deployed and experimental FL systems,
including the Flower-based setup employed in this thesis.

\subsection{Client-Server Paradigm}
\label{sec:client-server}

The canonical FL architecture is organised as a two-tier hierarchy
consisting of a central coordination server and a set of $K$ clients,
each in possession of a private local dataset $\mathcal{D}_k$ that
remains on-site throughout the entire training process
\cite{mcmahan2017communication}.
The server is the orchestrating entity: it maintains the authoritative
copy of the global model, schedules training rounds, selects
participating clients, aggregates locally computed updates, and
broadcasts the updated global model.
Clients are the computational entities: each executes local
optimisation on its own data and returns parameter updates to the
server without exposing raw records.

This division of responsibility reflects both the privacy requirement
and the typical infrastructure of cross-silo healthcare deployments.
The server may be hosted by a neutral third party -- a research
consortium, a cloud provider operating under a data-processing
agreement, or one of the participating hospitals designated as
coordinator -- provided that the server never receives raw patient data.
Clients in a cross-silo setting are typically individual hospital sites
or research centres, each equipped with sufficient GPU compute to
execute local training on their imaging datasets
\cite{rieke2020future,kairouz2021advances}.

An important architectural distinction concerns the degree of client
selection per round.
In large-scale cross-device FL, only a small fraction of available
clients participate in each round due to device availability and
bandwidth constraints.
In cross-silo deployments of the kind considered in this thesis, where
the total number of clients is small (three simulated hospital nodes),
all clients participate in every round, simplifying convergence
analysis and eliminating the client-dropout variability that
complicates cross-device FL \cite{kairouz2021advances}.

\subsection{Local Training at Client Institutions}
\label{sec:local-training}

At the commencement of each communication round $t$, every selected
client $k \in \mathcal{S}^{(t)}$ receives the current global parameter
vector $\mathbf{w}^{(t)}$ from the server and initialises its local
model with this shared starting point.
The client then performs a fixed number $E$ of local optimisation
steps using mini-batch stochastic gradient descent (SGD) over its
private dataset $\mathcal{D}_k$, minimising the local empirical risk
$F_k(\mathbf{w})$ defined in equation~\eqref{eq:local-risk}.

The choice of $E$ governs the trade-off between communication
efficiency and optimisation quality.
A single local step ($E = 1$) reduces to synchronous distributed SGD
and requires a communication round after every gradient computation,
incurring high bandwidth costs.
Multiple local steps ($E > 1$) amortise the communication overhead
across more computation but introduce \emph{client drift}: because
each client optimises its local objective rather than the global one,
the local model moves away from the global optimum at a rate that
depends on the statistical heterogeneity of the local data distribution
\cite{kairouz2021advances}.
In the non-IID setting characteristic of multi-hospital deployments --
where patient demographics, scanner protocols, and subtype prevalence
differ across sites -- client drift is a primary source of convergence
degradation and motivates the regularisation techniques discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:aggregation}.

In the breast MRI molecular subtype classification scenario of this
thesis, each client applies the same ResNet-based backbone architecture
initialised from the global weights, performing local updates using a
cosine learning rate schedule with MixUp augmentation to mitigate the
class imbalance arising from the non-IID partition of the 737-patient
dataset across three hospital nodes.
Progressive unfreezing of backbone layers during local training is
employed to preserve low-level feature representations learned from the
global model while adapting higher-level features to site-specific data
characteristics.

\subsection{Global Model Aggregation on the Server}
\label{sec:global-aggregation}

Upon completion of local training, each participating client $k$
transmits its updated local parameter vector $\mathbf{w}_k^{(t+1)}$
to the central server.
The server's aggregation function combines these client updates into a
new global model that, under the weighted averaging scheme of FedAvg,
corresponds to the sample-size-weighted mean of local parameters as
given in equation~\eqref{eq:fedavg-aggregation}.
The resulting global model $\mathbf{w}^{(t+1)}$ integrates knowledge
from all participating clients without requiring any client to share
its underlying data.

The correctness of weighted averaging as an aggregation operator rests
on the assumption that local models are trained from the same
initialisation and that the global objective is the weighted sum of
local objectives with weights proportional to dataset size
\cite{mcmahan2017communication}.
Under these conditions, averaging in parameter space approximates a
single gradient step on the global objective.
When the assumption of identical data distributions across clients is
violated -- the non-IID case -- this approximation degrades, and the
aggregated model may converge to a point that is suboptimal for all
clients simultaneously.
Alternative aggregation strategies that address this degradation are
surveyed in Section~\ref{sec:aggregation}.

A practical consideration in cross-silo medical imaging deployments is
the size of the parameter vectors being transmitted.
A ResNet-50 backbone comprises approximately 25 million floating-point
parameters; transmitting full parameter vectors from three hospital
clients per round is feasible within the bandwidth constraints of
institutional networks, in contrast to the gradient-compression
requirements of cross-device deployments involving thousands of
participants \cite{kairouz2021advances,rieke2020future}.

\subsection{Communication Rounds and Convergence}
\label{sec:comm-rounds}

Training in federated learning is structured as a sequence of discrete
communication rounds rather than the continuous gradient flow of
centralised training.
Each round consists of four steps: the server broadcasts the current
global model to selected clients; clients perform local optimisation;
clients transmit updated parameters to the server; and the server
aggregates the updates and prepares the next global model.
This round structure introduces a fundamental coupling between the
number of communication rounds $T$, the number of local steps $E$ per
round, and the total computational budget available to each client.

Convergence analysis for FedAvg under non-IID data distributions
demonstrates that the algorithm converges at a rate that degrades as
the degree of data heterogeneity -- measured by the gradient divergence
$\|\nabla F_k(\mathbf{w}) - \nabla F(\mathbf{w})\|$ across clients --
increases \cite{kairouz2021advances}.
Intuitively, when local data distributions differ substantially, local
gradient directions point towards different local optima, and their
average after multiple local steps is a poor approximation to the
global gradient.
The practical consequence is that non-IID FL systems may require
significantly more communication rounds to achieve a given target
accuracy than an equivalent centralised system trained on the pooled
dataset.

In healthcare FL deployments, the number of communication rounds is
also constrained by institutional factors beyond pure optimisation
considerations: each round involves synchronisation across hospital
sites that may operate on different schedules, under different
computational loads, and subject to network availability constraints
that do not arise in a single data centre.
For the three-hospital simulation in this thesis, all clients are
available synchronously, so round scheduling reduces to a hyperparameter
choice; the interaction between round count, local epochs, and
convergence on the non-IID breast MRI partition is reported in
Chapter~3.

\section{Aggregation Strategies}
\label{sec:aggregation}
% ============================================================

The aggregation step is the defining operation of federated learning:
it is the mechanism by which knowledge distributed across client
institutions is consolidated into a single global model.
The choice of aggregation strategy directly affects convergence speed,
final model accuracy, robustness to heterogeneous data distributions,
and the degree of fairness across clients with unequal dataset sizes
\cite{arbaoui2025survey}.
While Federated Averaging introduced a simple and effective baseline,
subsequent work has identified systematic failure modes of this baseline
under non-identically distributed data and proposed a range of
algorithmic corrections.
This section surveys the principal aggregation strategies relevant to
the cross-silo medical imaging context of this thesis, and concludes
with a comparative analysis.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{FedAvg: Federated Averaging}
\label{sec:fedavg}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Federated Averaging, proposed by McMahan~et~al.\ in 2017, remains the
foundational aggregation algorithm against which all subsequent methods
are benchmarked \cite{mcmahan2017communication}.
Its server-side rule, restated from equation~\eqref{eq:fedavg-aggregation},
aggregates client parameter vectors by forming a dataset-size-weighted
arithmetic mean:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{w}^{(t+1)}
  = \sum_{k \in \mathcal{S}^{(t)}}
    \frac{n_k}{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}^{(t)}} n_j}\,
    \mathbf{w}_k^{(t+1)}.
  \label{eq:fedavg-2}
\end{equation}
The principal hyperparameters controlling FedAvg are the fraction of
clients selected per round $C \in (0, 1]$, the number of local epochs
$E$, and the local mini-batch size $B$.
McMahan~et~al.\ showed empirically that increasing $E$ and decreasing $B$
reduces the number of communication rounds required to reach a target
accuracy by one to two orders of magnitude compared to naive
federated stochastic gradient descent, making computation more
efficient relative to communication \cite{mcmahan2017communication}.

Despite its simplicity and strong performance under
independent and identically distributed (IID) data, FedAvg exhibits
a well-documented weakness when client data distributions are
heterogeneous.
When local objectives $F_k$ diverge substantially, as is the case in
multi-hospital breast MRI classification where subtype prevalences
differ across institutions, the local gradient directions point towards
different local optima.
Performing multiple local SGD steps before aggregation causes the
local models to drift away from the global optimum, a phenomenon
termed \emph{client drift} \cite{kairouz2021advances,arbaoui2025survey}.
The weighted average of drifted models may not approximate the globally
optimal parameters, resulting in degraded convergence and lower final
accuracy under non-IID distributions.
The algorithms described in the following subsections were designed
specifically to correct this shortcoming.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.55\linewidth]{figures/mcmahan2017_algorithm1.png}
  \caption{The FederatedAveraging (FedAvg) algorithm.
    The server coordinates rounds and aggregates client updates;
    each client runs $E$ local epochs of SGD on its private dataset
    before returning updated parameters $\mathbf{w}_k^{(t+1)}$.
    Reproduced from \cite{mcmahan2017communication}.}
  \label{fig:fedavg-algorithm}
\end{figure}


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{FedProx}
\label{sec:fedprox}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

FedProx, proposed by Li~et~al.\ \cite{li2020fedprox}, addresses client
drift by modifying the local training objective rather than the
server-side aggregation rule.
Each client minimises a proximal regularised loss that penalises
deviation of the local parameters from the received global model:
\begin{equation}
  \min_{\mathbf{w}_k}\;
  F_k(\mathbf{w}_k)
  + \frac{\mu}{2}\,
  \bigl\lVert \mathbf{w}_k - \mathbf{w}^{(t)} \bigr\rVert^2,
  \label{eq:fedprox}
\end{equation}
where $\mu \geq 0$ is the proximal coefficient and $\mathbf{w}^{(t)}$
is the global model received at the start of round $t$.
The added quadratic term acts as an anchor that keeps the local update
close to the global starting point, limiting the degree of drift
regardless of how heterogeneous the local data distribution is.
When $\mu = 0$, equation~\eqref{eq:fedprox} reduces to the standard
FedAvg local objective, making FedAvg a special case of FedProx.

The server-side aggregation in FedProx is identical to FedAvg: the
global model is updated by the weighted average of the locally trained
parameter vectors.
The benefit of FedProx lies entirely in the improved quality of the
local updates delivered to the server.
Li~et~al.\ proved that FedProx converges to a neighbourhood of the
global optimum under general non-IID data distributions, providing
the first theoretical convergence guarantee for federated optimisation
in the heterogeneous setting \cite{li2020fedprox}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.80\linewidth]{figures/li2020_fedprox_convergence.png}
  \caption{Effect of data heterogeneity on convergence for FedAvg
    and FedProx across synthetic non-IID partitions.
    As the degree of statistical heterogeneity increases, FedAvg
    convergence degrades substantially, while the proximal
    regularisation term in FedProx stabilises the training curve.
    Adapted from \cite{li2020fedprox}.}
  \label{fig:fedprox-convergence}
\end{figure}

In practice, $\mu$ is a hyperparameter that controls the strength of
the proximal correction: large values of $\mu$ force local models to
stay close to the global model at the cost of slowing local adaptation,
while small values permit more local drift and approach the behaviour
of FedAvg.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{FedNova}
\label{sec:fednova}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

FedNova (Federated Normalised Averaging) was proposed by Li~et~al.\
\cite{li2020fednova} to address a subtle but consequential problem
in FedAvg known as \emph{objective inconsistency}.
When clients perform different numbers of local update steps, either
because they have datasets of different sizes or because they use
different batch sizes, the weighted average computed by FedAvg no
longer corresponds to a step in the direction of the gradient of the
global objective.
The updates from clients that performed more local steps are implicitly
given higher effective weight in the aggregate, biasing convergence
away from the globally optimal solution even when data are IID
\cite{li2020fednova}.

FedNova corrects this inconsistency by normalising each client's
update before aggregation.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.76\linewidth]{figures/li2020_fednova_inconsistency.png}
  \caption{Illustration of the objective inconsistency problem in
    FedAvg when clients perform different numbers of local update
    steps.
    The aggregated update direction deviates from the true global
    gradient direction.
    FedNova eliminates this bias by normalising each client's
    update by its local step count before aggregation.
    Adapted from \cite{li2020fednova}.}
  \label{fig:fednova-inconsistency}
\end{figure}

Let $\tau_k$ denote the number of local update steps performed by
client $k$ in a given round.
Instead of aggregating the raw local model $\mathbf{w}_k^{(t+1)}$,
the server aggregates the normalised update:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{d}_k
  = \frac{1}{\tau_k}
    \bigl(\mathbf{w}^{(t)} - \mathbf{w}_k^{(t+1)}\bigr),
  \label{eq:fednova-update}
\end{equation}
and the new global model is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{w}^{(t+1)}
  = \mathbf{w}^{(t)}
    - \eta_g \sum_{k \in \mathcal{S}^{(t)}}
      \frac{n_k}{\sum_{j} n_j}\, \tau_k\, \mathbf{d}_k,
  \label{eq:fednova-agg}
\end{equation}
where $\eta_g$ is a global learning rate.
This normalisation decouples the aggregation weight of each client
from the number of local steps it performed, restoring consistency
with the global objective and eliminating the convergence bias
identified in FedAvg \cite{li2020fednova,kairouz2021advances}.
FedNova is particularly relevant to cross-silo healthcare scenarios
where client hospitals may have substantially different dataset sizes
and thus naturally perform different numbers of local gradient steps
per round.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{SCAFFOLD}
\label{sec:scaffold}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

SCAFFOLD (Stochastic Controlled Averaging for Federated Learning)
was introduced by Karimireddy~et~al.\ \cite{karimireddy2020scaffold}
with the explicit goal of eliminating client drift through a
\emph{variance reduction} mechanism.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.78\linewidth]{figures/karimireddy2020_scaffold_drift.png}
  \caption{Client drift in FedAvg illustrated for two clients with
    heterogeneous data distributions.
    Each client's local SGD steps pull its model toward its own
    local optimum, and the weighted average of the two drifted
    models is a poor approximation of the global optimum.
    SCAFFOLD corrects this drift using client-level control variates.
    Adapted from \cite{karimireddy2020scaffold}.}
  \label{fig:scaffold-drift}
\end{figure}

Where FedProx limits drift implicitly by penalising deviation from
the global model, SCAFFOLD eliminates drift explicitly by maintaining
control variates at both the server and each client.

Let $\mathbf{c}$ denote the server control variate and
$\mathbf{c}_k$ the control variate of client $k$.
During local training, the client corrects its gradient estimate at
each step by subtracting the local variate and adding the server
variate:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{w}_k \leftarrow
  \mathbf{w}_k
  - \eta \bigl(
      \nabla F_k(\mathbf{w}_k)
      - \mathbf{c}_k
      + \mathbf{c}
    \bigr).
  \label{eq:scaffold-update}
\end{equation}
After local training, the client computes a corrected update for its
own control variate:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{c}_k^{+}
  = \mathbf{c}_k
    - \mathbf{c}
    + \frac{1}{E\eta}
      \bigl(\mathbf{w}^{(t)} - \mathbf{w}_k^{(t+1)}\bigr),
  \label{eq:scaffold-variate}
\end{equation}
and transmits both $\mathbf{w}_k^{(t+1)}$ and
$\mathbf{c}_k^{+} - \mathbf{c}_k$ to the server.
The server aggregates model updates and variate updates separately,
updating the global model by the mean of client model submissions and
the global variate by the mean of client variate corrections.

Karimireddy~et~al.\ proved that SCAFFOLD converges at the same
asymptotic rate as centralised SGD regardless of the degree of data
heterogeneity, a stronger guarantee than either FedAvg or FedProx
\cite{karimireddy2020scaffold}.
The cost of this guarantee is additional communication per round:
each client must transmit both its updated model and its updated
control variate.
In cross-silo settings where communication bandwidth is less
constrained than in cross-device settings, this overhead is generally
acceptable \cite{kairouz2021advances}.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Personalised Federated Learning}
\label{sec:personalised-fl}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The aggregation algorithms described in the preceding subsections
all pursue a single shared global model as their training objective.
In federated medical imaging, however, the goal of a single model
that performs uniformly across all participating institutions may
be neither achievable nor desirable.
If data distributions across hospitals differ fundamentally, for
instance because patient demographics, scanner hardware, and imaging
protocols vary substantially, forcing all local models to converge
to a common parameter vector may result in a model that performs
adequately on average but poorly at each individual site
\cite{kairouz2021advances,ghosh2020clustered}.

Personalised federated learning (PFL) relaxes the single-model
objective by allowing each client to maintain its own local model
that is informed by, but not identical to, the global model.
Several strategies have been proposed to achieve this.
In the \emph{local fine-tuning} approach, each client takes the
aggregated global model and performs additional local gradient steps
on its own data before deployment, adapting the global representation
to its local distribution without disrupting the federated training
process \cite{kairouz2021advances}.
In \emph{mixture} approaches, a convex combination of the global
model and the client's own locally trained model is used as the
final deployment model, with the mixing coefficient treated as a
hyperparameter \cite{arbaoui2025survey}.
More recent work employs meta-learning formulations in which the
global model is trained to serve as a good initialisation for
rapid local adaptation, a strategy well suited to cross-silo
scenarios where each client's distribution is stable over time
\cite{ghosh2020clustered}.

In the breast MRI molecular subtype classification task studied in
this thesis, the three simulated hospital clients hold partitions
with different subtype prevalences.
A personalised federated approach that permits per-hospital adaptation
after global aggregation is therefore a natural candidate for
improving per-site classification accuracy relative to the FedAvg
baseline, and is included in the experimental comparison reported
in Chapter~4.

\section{Communication Protocols and Optimization}
\label{sec:communication}
% ============================================================

Communication cost is one of the primary practical constraints in
federated learning.
Each communication round requires the server to broadcast the global
model to selected clients and to receive an updated model vector from
each of them.
For deep convolutional networks with millions of parameters, as are
typical in medical image classification, a single round may involve
transferring hundreds of megabytes per client.
Over tens to hundreds of rounds, the cumulative bandwidth consumption
can become prohibitive, particularly in cross-silo healthcare settings
where hospital network infrastructure may be shared with clinical
operations and subject to strict quality-of-service constraints
\cite{kairouz2021advances,arbaoui2025survey}.
This section surveys the sources of communication overhead in FL and
the principal techniques that have been proposed to reduce it.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Communication Overhead in Federated Learning}
\label{sec:comm-overhead}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

In a standard FedAvg run with $K$ clients and model dimensionality
$d$, each communication round requires the server to transmit
$d$ floating-point values to each selected client and to receive
$d$ values in return.
For a ResNet-50 model with approximately 25 million parameters
stored as 32-bit floats, a single round involves roughly 100 MB of
upload and download traffic per client.
With three hospital clients and 100 training rounds, the aggregate
communication volume already exceeds 60 GB, an amount that may
saturate clinical network links and incur measurable latency
\cite{mcmahan2017communication}.

Two distinct bottlenecks contribute to this overhead.
The \emph{uplink bottleneck} arises because hospital clients
typically operate on asymmetric network connections where upload
bandwidth is considerably smaller than download bandwidth.
The \emph{round-trip latency bottleneck} arises because synchronous
FL requires the server to wait for all selected clients to complete
their local training and return their updates before proceeding to
the next round; any client that is slow or temporarily unavailable
delays the entire round.
McMahan~et~al.\ showed that increasing the number of local epochs
$E$ per round substantially reduces the total number of rounds
required to achieve a given accuracy, thereby reducing the total
communication volume by up to two orders of magnitude relative to
communicating after every local gradient step
\cite{mcmahan2017communication}.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Gradient Compression Techniques}
\label{sec:compression}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Gradient and model compression techniques reduce the volume of data
transmitted per round by exploiting redundancy in the parameter
updates.
Kone\v{c}n\'y~et~al.\ introduced two complementary approaches:
\emph{structured updates}, in which the client constrains its local
update to a low-dimensional subspace parameterised by a random
matrix, and \emph{sketched updates}, in which the full update is
computed and then compressed using a lossy encoding scheme before
transmission \cite{konecny2016federated}.
Both approaches reduce uplink communication at the cost of a slight
degradation in model quality per round, a trade-off that is typically
acceptable given the large reduction in bandwidth consumption.

Subsequent work has extended these ideas to a variety of compression
operators, including random subsampling of coordinates, random rotation
followed by subsampling, and low-rank matrix factorisation
\cite{kairouz2021advances}.
In the medical imaging context, the high dimensionality of convolutional
network parameters makes compression particularly attractive.
However, compression must be applied with care in federated settings:
naive compression of client updates can introduce systematic bias
into the aggregated gradient, slowing or destabilising convergence.
Error-feedback mechanisms, which accumulate the compression error at
the client and add it to the next round's update, have been shown to
eliminate this bias and restore convergence rates comparable to
uncompressed training \cite{kairouz2021advances}.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\linewidth]{figures/konecny2016_compression.png}
  \caption{Structured and sketched update strategies for reducing
    client-to-server communication in federated learning.
    Structured updates constrain the local update to a low-dimensional
    subspace; sketched updates compute the full update and then apply
    lossy compression before transmission.
    Both approaches achieve substantial bandwidth reduction with

  \label{fig:konecny-compression}
\end{figure}


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Quantization and Sparsification}
\label{sec:quantization}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Quantization reduces communication cost by representing each
parameter or gradient value with fewer bits than the standard
32-bit floating-point format.
\emph{Stochastic quantization} replaces each real-valued coordinate
with a randomly rounded integer from a discrete codebook, producing
an unbiased estimate of the original value with substantially reduced
storage and transmission requirements
\cite{konecny2016federated,kairouz2021advances}.
For example, reducing from 32-bit to 8-bit fixed-point representation
yields a fourfold reduction in upload volume with minimal impact on
model accuracy when applied after a brief warm-up phase of full
precision training.

Sparsification complements quantization by transmitting only a
subset of parameter coordinates per round.
In \emph{top-$k$ sparsification}, each client computes its full
local update and then retains only the $k$ coordinates with the
largest absolute values, transmitting a sparse vector that encodes
both the indices and values of the selected coordinates.
With $k \ll d$, the reduction in communication volume can be
substantial.
As with compression, error feedback is essential to prevent the
dropped coordinates from permanently biasing the global model
\cite{kairouz2021advances}.
In the breast MRI classification setting of this thesis, model
updates are transmitted without compression or quantization to
maintain numerical stability during the experimental evaluation;
compression is identified as a direction for future work in
Chapter~5.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Synchronous versus Asynchronous Communication}
\label{sec:sync-async}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The canonical FedAvg protocol operates synchronously: the server
waits for all selected clients to return their updates before
computing the aggregate and proceeding to round $t+1$.
Synchronous aggregation is straightforward to implement and analyse
theoretically, but it is sensitive to \emph{stragglers}, clients
that take significantly longer than others to complete local training
due to hardware limitations, network congestion, or large local
datasets \cite{arbaoui2025survey}.
In cross-silo healthcare settings where all hospital clients are
dedicated institutional machines, straggler effects are less severe
than in cross-device settings, but they remain a source of idle
time at the server.

Asynchronous aggregation addresses this limitation by allowing the
server to update the global model as soon as any client returns its
update, without waiting for the remaining clients.
The server maintains a version counter and applies each client's
update to whichever version of the global model is current at the
time of receipt.
Asynchronous protocols can improve hardware utilisation and reduce
wall-clock training time, but they introduce \emph{staleness}: a
client that trained on model version $t$ may return its update
when the server has already advanced to version $t + s$, and
applying a stale update can introduce noise into the global model
\cite{arbaoui2025survey,kairouz2021advances}.

Semi-synchronous and semi-asynchronous protocols interpolate between
the two extremes by setting a deadline after which the server
aggregates whatever updates have arrived and discards the rest.
This \emph{bounded staleness} approach limits the impact of
stragglers while retaining most of the theoretical guarantees of
synchronous aggregation.
As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:aggregation-classification}, the
synchronisation mode is one of four axes along which FL aggregation
methods can be classified, alongside federation scale, network
topology, and federation motivation \cite{arbaoui2025survey}.
In this thesis, synchronous aggregation is used throughout the
experimental evaluation, consistent with the cross-silo setting
where all three hospital clients are assumed to be persistently
available.

\section{Privacy and Security in Federated Learning}
\label{sec:privacy-security}
% ============================================================

Federated learning was designed with privacy in mind: by keeping
raw patient data at each client institution, it removes the most
direct route through which sensitive information could be exposed.
However, the model parameters exchanged between clients and server
are not informationally neutral; they encode statistical properties
of the training data and can, under certain conditions, be exploited
by a party seeking to infer private information
\cite{kaissis2020secure}.
Understanding the threat landscape and the cryptographic and
statistical mechanisms available to counter it is essential for
deploying FL responsibly in a clinical context.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Threat Models}
\label{sec:threat-models}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Kaissis~et~al.\ provide a comprehensive taxonomy of attack vectors
relevant to machine learning in medical imaging, reproduced in
simplified form in Figure~\ref{fig:kaissis-privacy}
\cite{kaissis2020secure}.
Attacks can be directed against the dataset or against the algorithm.

Attacks against the dataset include \emph{re-identification attacks},
in which an adversary determines an individual's identity from
anonymised records using auxiliary information; \emph{dataset
reconstruction attacks}, in which characteristics of training examples
are derived from aggregate statistics without direct dataset access;
and \emph{membership inference attacks}, in which an adversary
determines whether a specific individual's data was used in training
\cite{kaissis2020secure}.
In the federated setting, these attacks can be carried out by a
curious server that inspects the parameter updates received from
clients, or by a malicious client that observes the updates broadcast
by the server.

Attacks against the algorithm include \emph{model inversion attacks},
in which private training data are reconstructed from the learned
model weights by solving an optimisation problem in input space;
\emph{data poisoning attacks}, in which a malicious client
injects corrupted training examples to degrade global model accuracy
or introduce targeted misclassification; and \emph{model poisoning
attacks}, in which a malicious client directly manipulates its
uploaded parameter vector rather than its training data
\cite{kaissis2020secure,kairouz2021advances}.
The free-rider attack is a specific form of model poisoning in which
a client uploads uninformative or random updates to the server while
benefiting from the global model without contributing genuine
gradients.

\subsection{Differential Privacy}
\label{sec:differential-privacy}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Differential privacy (DP) provides a mathematically rigorous
framework for bounding the amount of information that any single
training example contributes to the learned model.
A randomised mechanism $\mathcal{M}$ satisfies
$(\varepsilon, \delta)$-differential privacy if, for any two
datasets differing in a single example and any output set $S$:
\begin{equation}
  \Pr\bigl[\mathcal{M}(\mathcal{D}) \in S\bigr]
  \;\leq\;
  e^{\varepsilon}\,
  \Pr\bigl[\mathcal{M}(\mathcal{D}') \in S\bigr]
  + \delta,
  \label{eq:dp}
\end{equation}
where $\varepsilon$ controls the privacy budget (smaller values
imply stronger privacy) and $\delta$ is the probability of a
catastrophic privacy failure \cite{abadi2016deep}.

In the federated setting, DP is typically implemented by clipping
the norm of each client's gradient update to a maximum value $C$
and then adding calibrated Gaussian noise before transmission:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{\mathbf{g}}_k
  = \frac{\mathbf{g}_k}{\max(1,\, \lVert \mathbf{g}_k \rVert_2 / C)}
    + \mathcal{N}(0,\, \sigma^2 C^2 \mathbf{I}),
  \label{eq:dp-noise}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ is the noise multiplier whose value determines the
privacy-utility trade-off \cite{abadi2016deep,kairouz2021advances}.
The clipping step bounds the sensitivity of the update to the
presence of any single training example, while the noise injection
prevents the server or any eavesdropper from inferring that
sensitivity from the observed update.
Larger values of $\sigma$ provide stronger privacy guarantees
at the cost of noisier gradients and slower convergence.
In medical imaging applications, where training datasets are
relatively small and each clinical case may be unique, the
noise-accuracy trade-off is particularly acute and requires
careful calibration \cite{kaissis2020secure}.

  \centering

  \caption{The DP-SGD algorithm of Abadi~et~al.\ Each training step
    clips the per-example gradient to bound its $\ell_2$ norm,
    then adds calibrated Gaussian noise before the parameter update.
    The privacy cost accumulates across steps and is tracked by the
    moments accountant, yielding an $(\varepsilon,\delta)$-DP guarantee
    at the end of training.
    Reproduced from \cite{abadi2016deep}.}
  \label{fig:dpsgd-algorithm}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Secure Aggregation}
\label{sec:secure-aggregation}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Secure aggregation protocols allow the server to compute the sum
or weighted average of client updates without observing any
individual client's update in the clear.
The protocol introduced by Bonawitz~et~al.\ uses a combination
of secret sharing and pairwise masking to ensure that the server
receives only the aggregate of all client updates, with each
individual contribution masked by random values that cancel
in the sum \cite{bonawitz2017practical}.

Formally, each pair of clients $(i, j)$ agrees on a shared random
mask $\mathbf{r}_{ij}$ using a Diffie-Hellman key exchange.
Client $i$ adds $\mathbf{r}_{ij}$ to its update for all $j > i$
and subtracts $\mathbf{r}_{ij}$ for all $j < i$.
When the server sums all masked updates, the pairwise masks
cancel exactly and the server recovers the unmasked aggregate,
but cannot attribute any portion of the aggregate to any individual
client \cite{bonawitz2017practical}.
The protocol is robust to dropout: if a client fails to upload
its update, the remaining clients can reconstruct the missing masks
using a threshold secret-sharing scheme.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.80\linewidth]{figures/bonawitz2017_secagg.png}
  \caption{Communication diagram for practical secure aggregation.
    Each pair of clients establishes a shared random mask via
    Diffie-Hellman key exchange; the masks cancel in the server's
    sum so that only the aggregate is revealed.
    The protocol tolerates client dropout through threshold
    secret sharing.
    Adapted from \cite{bonawitz2017practical}.}
  \label{fig:secagg-protocol}
\end{figure}

Secure aggregation is complementary to differential privacy:
DP provides statistical guarantees against inference from the
aggregate, while secure aggregation ensures that the server
never sees individual updates even in the clear.
Combining both mechanisms provides defence in depth appropriate
for clinical deployments \cite{kaissis2020secure,kairouz2021advances}.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Homomorphic Encryption}
\label{sec:homomorphic-enc}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Homomorphic encryption (HE) enables arithmetic operations to be
performed directly on ciphertext, so that the result of decrypting
the output equals what would have been obtained by performing the
same operation on the plaintext.
In the federated learning context, clients encrypt their model
updates before uploading them; the server aggregates the encrypted
values using the additive homomorphism property; and the resulting
aggregate is decrypted only once, by a designated party holding
the decryption key \cite{kaissis2020secure}.

The primary advantage of HE over masking-based secure aggregation
is that it provides information-theoretic rather than
computational security: even an adversary with unbounded
computational resources cannot infer anything about individual
updates from the ciphertext.
The primary disadvantage is computational cost: homomorphic
operations on large parameter vectors are orders of magnitude
slower than their plaintext equivalents, and the ciphertext
expansion factor can increase communication volume substantially
\cite{kairouz2021advances}.
Partially homomorphic schemes, which support either addition or
multiplication but not both, offer a practical compromise for
federated averaging, which requires only addition.
The adoption of HE in production federated medical imaging systems
remains limited but is an active area of research, particularly
for settings where the server cannot be assumed to be honest
\cite{kaissis2020secure}.

\section{Convergence and Stability Challenges}
\label{sec:convergence}
% ============================================================

Convergence in federated learning is substantially more complex
than in centralised training because the optimisation landscape
is shaped by the interaction between global aggregation and
local client objectives that differ from one another.
This section analyses the two principal sources of instability
in federated training: statistical heterogeneity arising from
non-IID data distributions, and system heterogeneity arising
from differences in client hardware and availability.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Statistical Heterogeneity: Non-IID Data}
\label{sec:non-iid}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The fundamental assumption underlying FedAvg is that the weighted
average of locally optimal parameter vectors approximates the globally
optimal parameter vector.
This approximation is accurate when client data distributions are
similar, but degrades as the distributions diverge.
When client data distributions are non-identical, the local objective
$F_k$ of each client has a different minimiser, and performing
multiple local gradient steps pushes the local model towards that
local minimiser and away from the global optimum.
The aggregated model, being a weighted combination of models that
have drifted in different directions, may not represent a good
solution to any client's local objective or to the global objective
\cite{kairouz2021advances,li2020fedprox}.

In the breast MRI molecular subtype classification scenario of this
thesis, non-IID conditions arise because the three simulated hospital
clients hold partitions of the dataset in which the four subtype
classes are represented with different relative frequencies.
Figure~\ref{fig:arbaoui-scales} illustrates the distinction between
cross-device and cross-silo federation, highlighting the greater
data heterogeneity typical of institutional silos relative to
consumer device distributions \cite{arbaoui2025survey}.
The degree of non-IID-ness can be parameterised by the Dirichlet
concentration parameter $\alpha$: as $\alpha \to 0$, each client
holds data from only one class; as $\alpha \to \infty$, the
distribution approaches the IID setting.
The experimental evaluation in Chapter~4 examines convergence
under the partition strategy employed for the 737-patient cohort.

  \centering

  \caption{%

  \label{fig:arbaoui-scales}
\end{figure}


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{System Heterogeneity}
\label{sec:system-heterogeneity}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

System heterogeneity refers to differences between clients in
computational capacity, memory, network bandwidth, and availability.
In cross-device settings, system heterogeneity is extreme: a
federation may include devices ranging from high-end smartphones
to low-end IoT sensors, and any device may become unavailable at
any time due to battery depletion or network disconnection
\cite{kairouz2021advances}.
In cross-silo healthcare settings, system heterogeneity is less
severe but not negligible: different hospital imaging departments
may operate different GPU generations, different storage
architectures, and different local preprocessing pipelines, all
of which affect the speed of local training and the volume of
data processed per round.

System heterogeneity interacts with statistical heterogeneity to
produce the straggler problem described in Section~\ref{sec:sync-async}:
the clients that take longest to complete local training are often
those with the smallest computational budgets, and these clients
may also have the most distinctive data distributions.
Excluding stragglers from aggregation to avoid blocking the
server therefore introduces a systematic bias, as the excluded
clients' data distributions are underrepresented in the global
model \cite{li2020fedprox,kairouz2021advances}.
FedProx addresses this by allowing partial work: clients that
cannot complete the full $E$ local epochs before a deadline may
submit their partially trained parameters, which are still
informative contributions to the proximal objective in
equation~\eqref{eq:fedprox}.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Strategies to Stabilise Convergence}
\label{sec:convergence-strategies}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Several complementary strategies have been developed to stabilise
FL convergence under heterogeneous conditions.

The proximal regularisation approach of FedProx and the
variance-reduction approach of SCAFFOLD were described in
Section~\ref{sec:aggregation}.
Beyond these, \emph{server-side momentum} has been shown to
accelerate convergence by applying momentum to the global
update direction across rounds, analogously to how momentum
accelerates stochastic gradient descent in centralised training
\cite{kairouz2021advances}.
\emph{Adaptive server optimisers}, such as applying Adam or
AdaGrad to the server-side update step, allow the global
learning rate to adapt to the curvature of the aggregated
gradient signal, improving stability when client updates are
noisy or heterogeneous \cite{kairouz2021advances}.

Careful \emph{data partitioning strategy} is also consequential.
When the practitioner controls how the dataset is partitioned
across simulated clients, choosing a partitioning that preserves
some class representation at each client, rather than assigning
entire class slices to individual clients, substantially reduces
the severity of client drift while still maintaining a realistic
degree of heterogeneity.
In Chapter~3, the partitioning strategy applied to the 737-patient
breast MRI cohort is described and justified in terms of its
clinical plausibility.
Finally, \emph{local learning rate scheduling} within each
client round, specifically the use of cosine annealing, has been
shown to reduce the magnitude of client drift by progressively
reducing step sizes as local training approaches a local optimum,
thereby limiting the distance the local model drifts from the
global initialisation \cite{arbaoui2025survey}.

\section{Existing Federated Learning Frameworks}
\label{sec:frameworks}
% ============================================================

A growing ecosystem of open-source software frameworks has
emerged to support federated learning research and deployment,
abstracting the communication protocol, client management, and
aggregation logic from the application-specific model and
training code.
This section describes the four most widely adopted frameworks
in the research community and compares their suitability for
the cross-silo medical imaging scenario of this thesis.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Flower (Flwr)}
\label{sec:flower}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Flower is a flexible, framework-agnostic federated learning
library that abstracts the federation logic from the local
training code through a clean client-server interface
\cite{beutel2022flower}.
Clients implement a \texttt{NumPyClient} or \texttt{Client}
interface that defines how local training is performed, how
model parameters are serialised and deserialised, and how
evaluation is conducted on local data.
The server manages the communication protocol, client selection,
and aggregation strategy.
Flower supports any deep learning backend, including PyTorch,
TensorFlow, JAX, and scikit-learn, making it straightforward
to integrate with existing model codebases.

Flower natively implements FedAvg, FedProx, and several other
aggregation strategies as configurable server-side strategies,
and provides a simulation mode that allows large-scale FL
experiments to be conducted on a single machine without network
infrastructure.
Its lightweight design and clear separation of concerns between
the federation layer and the local training layer make it the
framework of choice for cross-silo research experiments
\cite{beutel2022flower}.
Flower is used throughout the experimental evaluation of this
thesis to orchestrate the three-hospital breast MRI federation.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{FedML}
\label{sec:fedml}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

FedML is a comprehensive federated learning research platform
that supports cross-device, cross-silo, and split learning
scenarios within a unified codebase \cite{kairouz2021advances}.
It provides built-in support for a wide range of aggregation
algorithms, including FedAvg, FedProx, FedNova, and SCAFFOLD,
as well as simulation and real distributed deployment modes.
FedML is designed for scalability and includes tools for
benchmarking across multiple datasets and model architectures.
Its higher complexity relative to Flower makes it better suited
to large-scale benchmark studies than to focused research
implementations.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Substra}
\label{sec:substra}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Substra is a federated learning framework developed with
healthcare and life sciences applications as its primary
motivation \cite{rieke2020future}.
Unlike Flower and FedML, which target research reproducibility,
Substra is designed for production deployment in regulated
clinical environments: it provides audit trails, data governance
controls, and permissioned access management compatible with
GDPR and HIPAA requirements.
Its architecture enforces data sovereignty by ensuring that raw
data never leaves the local node and that all computations on
local data are logged and auditable.
Substra has been used in multi-institutional studies involving
hospitals in France and the United Kingdom, where its compliance
features were essential for ethical approval \cite{kaissis2020secure}.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{PySyft}
\label{sec:pysyft}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

PySyft is a privacy-focused machine learning library built on
top of PyTorch that supports federated learning in combination
with differential privacy and secure multi-party computation
\cite{kaissis2020secure}.
Its tight integration with the PyTorch autograd system allows
gradient-level operations, including DP noise injection and
secure aggregation masking, to be inserted transparently into
the standard PyTorch training loop.
PySyft is particularly well suited to research scenarios that
combine FL with formal privacy guarantees, as it provides
implementations of the DP-SGD algorithm of Abadi~et~al.\
and of secret-sharing-based secure aggregation in a single
unified interface \cite{abadi2016deep}.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Federated Learning for Medical Image Analysis}
\label{sec:related-medical-imaging}

The application of federated learning to medical image analysis
has expanded rapidly since Sheller~et~al.\ demonstrated that a
federated brain tumour segmentation model trained across ten
institutions, without sharing any patient data, reached performance
within one percentage point of a centrally trained baseline
\cite{sheller2020federated}.
This foundational result established the clinical credibility of FL
as a collaborative learning paradigm and motivated a broad programme
of follow-on work spanning radiology, pathology, and oncology.
Guan~et~al.\ conducted a systematic survey of FL methods applied to
medical image analysis from 2017 to 2023, organising the field
around three system perspectives: client-side methods addressing
local training and data heterogeneity, server-side methods addressing
aggregation and personalisation, and communication methods addressing
bandwidth and round efficiency \cite{guan2024federated}.
Their survey provides the most comprehensive reference for locating
this thesis within the existing literature on FL for radiology and
oncology imaging.
Rieke~et~al.\ situated FL within the wider landscape of digital
health, arguing that federated approaches are necessary for building
AI systems that generalise across the diversity of clinical
environments worldwide \cite{rieke2020future}.
Kaissis~et~al.\ provided a rigorous taxonomy of privacy threats and
defences in medical imaging AI, establishing the conceptual framework
within which the security properties of FL systems are evaluated
\cite{kaissis2020secure}.

\subsection{Federated Learning for Breast Cancer and Oncology Imaging}
\label{sec:related-breast-cancer}

The most directly related work on federated learning for breast
cancer imaging is surveyed by Ghosh~et~al., who review federated
approaches across five cancer imaging modalities including breast MRI,
mammography, histopathology, and dermoscopy
\cite{ghosh2020clustered}.
Their survey identifies class imbalance, non-IID subtype distributions
across institutions, and the high dimensionality of volumetric
DCE-MRI data as the three principal challenges for federated breast
cancer classification, all of which are addressed by the methodology
developed in Chapter~3 of this thesis.
The Algerian contribution to FL aggregation research represented by
Arbaoui~et~al.\ \cite{arbaoui2025survey} provides the most current
empirical analysis of aggregation strategies under IID and non-IID
conditions, offering a direct benchmark for the aggregation comparison
conducted in Chapter~4.

\subsection{Positioning of This Work}
\label{sec:related-positioning}

To the best of the authors' knowledge, no prior published work has
applied federated learning to the molecular subtype classification
of breast DCE-MRI using the publicly available Zenodo dataset
\cite{alves2023breastmri} with the combination of
class-imbalance correction through weighted sampling, progressive
backbone unfreezing, and a systematic comparison of FedAvg and
SCAFFOLD aggregation strategies under non-IID conditions adopted
in this thesis.
The present work therefore contributes an original experimental
evaluation of cross-silo federated learning for a clinically
significant breast DCE-MRI classification task under binary
and four-class formulations under realistic non-IID conditions.

\section{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:problem-statement}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Motivation}
\label{sec:ps-motivation}

The clinical deployment of deep learning systems for medical image analysis is
fundamentally constrained by the fragmentation of patient data across healthcare
institutions.
Regulatory frameworks governing the processing of health records --- most notably
the General Data Protection Regulation in the European Union
\cite{gdpr2016regulation}, the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability
Act in the United States \cite{hipaa1996health}, and Law~18-07 of
June~10,~2018 in Algeria --- impose strict conditions on any
cross-institutional transfer of protected health information, conditions that a
conventional centralised training pipeline cannot satisfy without substantial legal
and administrative overhead.
In Algeria, the same obligations apply under
Law~18-07, which governs any processing of health data
by Algerian institutions regardless of whether the data
are transferred abroad.
As a consequence, the large, diverse, and representative datasets that deep
learning models require for reliable generalisation are, in practice, inaccessible
through centralisation: each institution's imaging archive constitutes an isolated
silo that can neither be pooled with nor directly compared against those of
neighbouring sites.

Breast cancer molecular subtype classification from dynamic contrast-enhanced MRI
exemplifies this tension.
Accurately distinguishing Luminal~A, Luminal~B, HER2-enriched, and Triple-Negative
subtypes from volumetric DCE-MRI data is clinically consequential --- subtype
assignment directly determines therapeutic strategy --- yet the inter-institutional
variability in scanner hardware, acquisition protocol, patient demographics, and
subtype prevalence means that models trained at a single site are unlikely to
generalise to other clinical environments \cite{ghosh2020clustered}.
Federated learning offers a principled mechanism for exploiting multi-institutional
data under these constraints by exchanging only model parameter updates rather than
raw patient records.
To the best of the authors' knowledge, no prior published work has applied federated
learning to molecular subtype classification on the publicly available breast
DCE-MRI cohort \cite{alves2023breastmri} with a variance-reduction aggregation
strategy such as SCAFFOLD, leaving a significant gap in the empirical literature
that this thesis addresses.


\subsection{Research Questions}
\label{sec:ps-research-questions}

This thesis is organised around three research questions that progress from
centralised baseline characterisation to federated optimisation:

\begin{enumerate}

  \item[\textbf{RQ1.}] What classification performance ceiling does a centralised
  deep learning system achieve on breast DCE-MRI molecular subtype prediction
  under severe class imbalance, and how does a clinically motivated binary
  reformulation address the data scarcity constraint imposed by rare subtypes?

  \item[\textbf{RQ2.}] How much classification performance is lost when training
  is distributed across three simulated hospital clients with non-IID data
  partitioning under the FedAvg aggregation strategy, and what is the
  quantitative privacy-performance trade-off?

  \item[\textbf{RQ3.}] Can SCAFFOLD's control-variate correction mechanism reduce
  the convergence gap caused by statistical heterogeneity in a three-hospital
  federated breast MRI setting, and to what extent does convergence-aware
  aggregation recover performance lost to client drift?

\end{enumerate}


\subsection{Formal Hypotheses}
\label{sec:ps-hypotheses}

The three research questions yield the following testable hypotheses:

\begin{enumerate}

  \item[\textbf{H1.}] A ConvNeXt-Nano backbone with Gated Attention MIL, trained
  with LDAM loss and ASAM optimisation, achieves higher macro F1 on the four-class
  centralised task than a standard cross-entropy baseline, because the combination
  of margin-based loss and sharpness-aware minimisation better handles the
  10.4:1 class imbalance present in the dataset.

  \item[\textbf{H2.}] FedAvg aggregation across three non-IID hospital clients
  incurs a performance penalty relative to the centralised baseline, because local
  gradient updates on heterogeneous data distributions cause client drift that is
  not corrected by simple weighted averaging.

  \item[\textbf{H3.}] SCAFFOLD's control-variate mechanism reduces the client
  drift identified in~H2 and narrows the centralised-federated performance gap,
  because it explicitly estimates and corrects the client-specific gradient bias
  at each communication round.

\end{enumerate}


\subsection{Thesis Contributions}
\label{sec:ps-contributions}

This thesis makes the following original contributions to the federated medical
imaging literature:

\begin{enumerate}

  \item[\textbf{C1.}] A three-stage centralised training pipeline --- combining
  Label-Distribution-Aware Margin loss, Sharpness-Aware Minimisation, Stochastic
  Weight Averaging, and classifier re-training (LDAM + ASAM + SWA + cRT) --- for
  breast DCE-MRI molecular subtype classification under severe class imbalance,
  evaluated over five-fold cross-validation with patient-level stratification.

  \item[\textbf{C2.}] A clinically motivated binary reformulation of the molecular
  subtype classification task that maps directly to the hormone therapy eligibility
  decision boundary, demonstrated to be more tractable under federated constraints
  than the four-class formulation.

  \item[\textbf{C3.}] An empirical comparison of FedAvg and SCAFFOLD aggregation
  strategies under three degrees of non-IID data heterogeneity
  (Dirichlet $\alpha \in \{0.1,\, 0.5,\, 100\}$) on the publicly available breast
  DCE-MRI dataset \cite{alves2023breastmri}, quantifying the convergence benefit
  of control-variate correction.

  \item[\textbf{C4.}] A proof-of-concept radiomic feature fusion experiment
  combining hand-crafted PyRadiomics features with deep MIL embeddings, providing
  an interpretable complement to the end-to-end deep learning pipeline.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:ch2-conclusion}
% ============================================================

This chapter has provided a structured survey of federated
learning from its origins in the mobile cross-device setting
through to its application in cross-silo clinical imaging.
The central thread running through the survey is the tension
between the statistical benefits of multi-institutional
collaboration and the legal and ethical constraints on patient
data sharing; federated learning resolves this tension by
inverting the data-movement assumption and bringing the model
to the data rather than the reverse.

The canonical FedAvg algorithm and its principal variants,
FedProx, FedNova, and SCAFFOLD, have been described and
compared with respect to their convergence guarantees and
suitability for non-IID medical imaging data.
Communication protocols, privacy mechanisms, and convergence
challenges have been surveyed with specific attention to the
cross-silo, three-hospital breast MRI scenario that motivates
this thesis.
The Flower framework has been identified as the implementation
platform, and related work has positioned the present
contribution within the federated medical imaging literature.
Among the aggregation strategies surveyed,
SCAFFOLD \cite{karimireddy2020scaffold} is selected as
the optimisation variant for the federated experiment on
the grounds that its control-variate correction mechanism
directly targets the client drift caused by the non-IID
three-hospital data partition, which is the primary
convergence challenge identified in
Section~\ref{sec:convergence}.

Chapter~3 translates the conceptual and algorithmic framework
developed here into a concrete experimental design: the
breast MRI dataset is described, the non-IID partitioning
strategy is defined, the ConvNeXt-Nano backbone with Gated
Attention Multiple Instance Learning head is specified,
and the full federated training pipeline is detailed.
