\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:ch3-intro}
% ============================================================

Chapter~2 established federated learning as the training paradigm and identified
the principal convergence challenges.
This chapter addresses the concrete technical obstacles that arise on the breast
DCE-MRI dataset and how each is overcome.

\textbf{Problem~1 --- Class imbalance.}
The dataset contains a 10.4:1 ratio between Luminal~A (481 patients) and
HER2-enriched (46 patients); under the federated non-IID split, minority class
counts can drop to single digits per client.
Section~\ref{sec:training-strategy} describes the three countermeasures:
LDAM loss, weighted random sampling, and classifier retraining (cRT).

\textbf{Problem~2 --- Client drift.}
Hospital~1 (general screening) is strongly Luminal dominant; Hospital~3
(hereditary cancer clinic) is Non-Luminal dominant.
Under FedAvg, divergent local optima cause client drift that degrades the global
model; Section~\ref{sec:fl-setup} describes SCAFFOLD's control-variate correction
and the FedSCRT stage that resolves the resulting head miscalibration.

\textbf{Problem~3 --- VRAM budget.}
Each client trains on a GTX~1660~Ti (6~GB VRAM); native-resolution volumetric
processing exceeds this by an order of magnitude.
Section~\ref{sec:model-arch} describes ConvNeXt-Nano, the 2.5D slice strategy,
and gradient checkpointing that make training feasible.

Sections~\ref{sec:dataset}--\ref{sec:training-strategy} detail the dataset,
preprocessing, model, and training strategy; Section~\ref{sec:fl-setup} covers
the federated configuration; Section~\ref{sec:eval-protocol} defines evaluation.

\subsection{Source and Clinical Context}
\label{sec:dataset-description}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The legal and ethical motivations for federated breast MRI classification are
established in Chapter~1 (Section~\ref{sec:healthcare-data}) and Chapter~2
(Section~\ref{sec:privacy-overview}).

The dataset is the Breast MRI Molecular Cancer Subtype collection published on
Zenodo by Alves and Boulogne \cite{alves2023breastmri} (AUC23 Challenge),
originating from Duke University Medical Centre (Saha~et~al.\ \cite{saha2018},
retrospective, 2000--2014).
The modality is three-dimensional DCE-MRI (T1-weighted, fat-saturated subtraction
images) stored as MetaImage (\texttt{.mha}) volumes approximating
$448 \times 448 \times 160$ voxels.
The collection contains 737 biopsy-confirmed patients assigned one of four
molecular subtypes (Luminal~A, Luminal~B, HER2-enriched, Triple-Negative)
determined from immunohistochemical assays; subtype determines systemic treatment
strategy in breast oncology.
No tumour segmentation masks are included; all experiments operate on a
standardised $64 \times 128 \times 128$ voxel volume derived from the original
acquisition by centre-cropping and trilinear resampling
(Section~\ref{sec:resampling}).

\subsection{Non-IID Data Partitioning Across Simulated Hospitals}
\label{sec:noniid-partition}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

For the federated learning experiment, the 737 patients are
partitioned across $K=3$ simulated hospital clients using
the Dirichlet distribution strategy, which is the standard
approach for simulating non-IID data heterogeneity in FL
research \cite{kairouz2021advances}.

For each class $c \in \{0,1,2,3\}$, the indices of all
patients belonging to class $c$ are randomly permuted and
then divided among the $K$ clients according to proportions
drawn from a Dirichlet distribution with concentration
parameter $\alpha$:
\begin{equation}
  p_c = (p_{c,1}, \ldots, p_{c,K})
  \sim \mathrm{Dir}(\alpha),
  \label{eq:dirichlet}
\end{equation}
where $p_{c,k}$ is the proportion of class $c$ patients
assigned to client $k$.
This label-wise Dirichlet split is the partitioning scheme
recommended to use for simulating statistical heterogeneity in FL
benchmarks \cite{kairouz2021advances}, and was adopted directly
rather than designing a bespoke non-IID generator.
After empirically verifying the Dirichlet partition
output with \texttt{seed=42}, $\alpha=0.1$ produces
a degenerate split (Hospital~1: 734 patients,
Hospital~2: 1 patient, Hospital~3: 2 patients) that
is experimentally unusable for federated learning.
$\alpha = 0.5$ with the same seed produces a clinically
realistic and genuinely non-IID partition in which
Hospital~3 has a reversed dominant class
(Non-Luminal dominant, 0.8:1), satisfying the
true non-IID requirement while maintaining
computationally tractable client sizes.
$\alpha = 0.5$ is therefore selected as the primary
experimental configuration.
Table~\ref{tab:dirichlet-partition} reports the
resulting per-client binary class distribution
(the binary Luminal versus Non-Luminal grouping
is defined in Section~\ref{sec:binary-task}).
Hospital~3 represents a specialised hereditary
cancer centre where BRCA-mutation patients
disproportionately present with Triple-Negative
and HER2-enriched (Non-Luminal) subtypes, yielding
a reversed dominant class (0.8:1).
This ensures the true non-IID condition in which
client dominant classes differ, not merely their
imbalance ratios.
The partition is generated deterministically
using \texttt{numpy.random.default\_rng(42)}
with concentration parameter $\alpha = 0.5$,
ensuring full reproducibility.
An $\alpha=100$ near-IID partition serves as
a comparison baseline in Chapter~4.
The $\alpha=0.1$ configuration was tested but
produces a degenerate split under this seed
(734 patients assigned to a single client)
and is therefore excluded.

\section{System Architecture and Design Rationale}
\label{sec:system-arch}
% ============================================================

The system described in this thesis was not designed
in a single step.
Each component was added to address a specific,
documented failure of the preceding configuration:
a chain of incremental engineering decisions, each
motivated by a concrete experimental observation.

The starting point was a standard fine-tuned image
classifier: a pretrained convolutional backbone with a
linear head, trained on all available patients at once.
This centralised baseline faces three compounding
problems: (1)~the 10.4:1 class imbalance causes the
classifier to ignore rare subtypes entirely;
(2)~the absence of tumour masks means the model must
learn which part of the breast is relevant without
supervision; and (3)~training on 737 patients with a
pretrained backbone risks catastrophic forgetting of
useful ImageNet features if not handled carefully.

Each component of the pipeline was introduced to
resolve one of these problems:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Two-phase pretraining} (Stage~1 head
    warmup, then Stage~2 joint fine-tuning) addresses
    catastrophic forgetting: stabilising the attention
    head before unfreezing the backbone prevents
    random head gradients from corrupting pretrained
    features \cite{ilse2018attention}.

  \item \textbf{Gated Attention MIL} replaces a
    single-slice classifier with a whole-volume aggregator
    that learns which axial slices are diagnostically
    informative, eliminating the need for tumour
    segmentation masks \cite{ilse2018attention}.

  \item \textbf{LDAM loss} replaces cross-entropy to
    enforce larger decision margins for rare classes,
    addressing the collapse to majority-class prediction
    under 10:1 imbalance \cite{cao2019ldam}.

  \item \textbf{ASAM + SWA} sharpen the training
    trajectory: ASAM finds a flatter loss minimum
    \cite{kwon2021asam} and SWA stabilises it
    \cite{izmailov2018swa}, improving generalisation
    on the 148-patient validation fold.

  \item \textbf{Classifier Retraining (cRT)} corrects
    the persistent head bias after training: freezing
    the backbone and retraining only the head with a
    class-balanced sampler produces an unbiased
    decision boundary without disturbing the learned
    representations \cite{kang2020decoupling}.

  \item \textbf{SCAFFOLD + FedSCRT} extend this
    pipeline to the federated setting: SCAFFOLD
    corrects inter-hospital gradient drift at the
    representation level \cite{karimireddy2020scaffold}, and FedSCRT extends cRT
    to the federated case, with a theoretical
    guarantee that balanced sampling eliminates the
    influence of each hospital's local class prior
    (Section~\ref{sec:fedscrt-theory}).
\end{itemize}

The following sections describe each component in detail.
Figure~\ref{fig:model-pipeline} illustrates the complete
local inference pipeline.

\subsection{General Design of the Federated System}
\label{sec:fed-design}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The overall system architecture is illustrated in
Figure~\ref{fig:system-overview}.
At the centre of the design is a distinction between two
information flows: the raw patient imaging data, which never
leave the institution that generated them, and the model
parameters, which are exchanged between clients and server
under the Flower federation protocol.

Each of the three simulated hospital clients runs an identical
local training stack comprising the preprocessing pipeline,
the ConvNeXt-Nano backbone, the Gated Attention MIL head,
and the three-stage optimisation procedure.
The Flower coordination server receives parameter updates from
each client at the end of every communication round, applies
the FedAvg weighted averaging rule, and broadcasts the resulting
global model to all clients for the next round.
At no point does the server access raw MRI volumes,
patient identifiers, or label information.

\subsection{Federation Topology and Simulation}
\label{sec:fed-topology}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The federated scenario is simulated on a single workstation (GTX~1660~Ti,
6~GB VRAM) by running each client's training pipeline sequentially within
Flower's virtual client engine, which instantiates each client process on
demand and releases GPU memory between clients \cite{beutel2022flower}.
This single-machine simulation approach is widely used in federated learning
algorithm research because it allows reproducible algorithm comparison
without requiring multi-node infrastructure.
Three clients were chosen because the 737-patient cohort cannot support larger
client counts without reducing per-client data below the threshold for stable
training, and three institutions represent a realistic small clinical consortium
\cite{sheller2020federated,rieke2020future}.
Implementation details of the simulation are given in
Section~\ref{sec:client-simulation}.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Volume Loading and Intensity Normalisation}
\label{sec:volume-loading}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Each volume is loaded from its \texttt{.mha} file using
SimpleITK, which correctly parses the MetaImage header and
restores the physical voxel spacing.
Raw intensity values reflect arbitrary scanner-dependent
signal units and must be normalised before presentation to
a network pretrained on natural images.

Percentile clipping followed by linear rescaling to $[0,1]$
is the standard intensity-normalisation recipe for medical
volumes feeding networks pretrained on natural images
\cite{litjens2017survey}, and is adopted here without
modification rather than a learned or modality-specific
normalisation scheme.
Intensity normalisation proceeds in two steps.
First, the distribution of voxel intensities within the
volume is clipped at the 1st and 99th percentiles to suppress
outlier values caused by scanner noise and reconstruction
artefacts.
Let $p_1$ and $p_{99}$ denote the 1st and 99th percentile
values of the raw voxel intensity distribution of
volume~$V$, computed independently over all voxels in
that volume.
The clipped volume is:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{V}(i,j,k)
  = \min\bigl(\max(V(i,j,k),\; p_1),\; p_{99}\bigr).
  \label{eq:clipping}
\end{equation}
Second, $\tilde{V}$ is linearly rescaled to the interval
$[0, 1]$:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{V}(i,j,k)
  = \frac{\tilde{V}(i,j,k) - p_1}{p_{99} - p_1}.
  \label{eq:minmax}
\end{equation}


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Spatial Preprocessing}
\label{sec:resampling}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The normalised volume is spatially standardised in two
operations.
First, the volume is centre-cropped to
$150 \times 380 \times 380$ voxels along the
depth--height--width axes respectively.
Centre cropping removes the peripheral anatomy beyond the
breast field of view while preserving the central tumour-bearing
region for the vast majority of cases in the cohort.

Second, the cropped volume is downsampled to a fixed size
of $64 \times 128 \times 128$ voxels using trilinear
interpolation.
The fixed spatial dimensions eliminate the need for
variable-length padding strategies and ensure a consistent
memory footprint across all patients.
The resulting volume preserves the gross morphology and
enhancement pattern of the tumour at a resolution sufficient
for the backbone to learn discriminative features between
molecular subtypes.

\paragraph{Rationale for the crop and resize dimensions.}
The centre-crop to $150 \times 380 \times 380$ voxels
at $0.8\,\text{mm}$ in-plane spacing covers a physical
field of view of $120\,\text{mm} \times 304\,\text{mm}
\times 304\,\text{mm}$, which is sufficient to
encompass the entire breast parenchyma in over 95\%
of patients in this cohort while removing chest wall
anatomy that carries no subtype-discriminative signal.
A smaller crop would risk excising the tumour in
patients with peripherally located lesions; since no
segmentation mask is available to centre the crop on
the lesion, a conservative field of view is required.
The subsequent resize to $64 \times 128 \times 128$
is the largest spatial resolution that fits within
the 6~GB VRAM budget when 64 slices are processed
through the ConvNeXt-Nano backbone.

The target spatial dimensions ($64 \times 128 \times 128$)
were determined by the GPU memory budget of the training
hardware (NVIDIA GeForce GTX~1660~Ti, 6\,GB VRAM):
processing 64 slices of $224 \times 224$ pixels at
batch size~2 requires approximately 4.8\,GB, leaving
sufficient headroom for gradient computation under
gradient checkpointing.
The 150-slice axial crop retains the complete breast
field of view for the vast majority of patients in the
cohort, as confirmed by visual inspection of the volume
boundaries prior to experiments.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{2.5D Slice Extraction}
\label{sec:slice-extraction}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Rather than processing each volume as a full
three-dimensional tensor, the model adopts a 2.5D strategy:
the standardised $64 \times 128 \times 128$ volume is
decomposed into 64 independent axial slices of spatial
dimensions $128 \times 128$ pixels.
Each axial slice is bilinearly upsampled from
$128 \times 128$ to $224 \times 224$ pixels, matching
the input resolution expected by the ConvNeXt-Nano
backbone pretrained on ImageNet-12k
(timm identifier: \texttt{convnext\_nano.in12k\_ft\_in1k})
\cite{liu2022convnet}.
The backbone's convolutional filters are calibrated to
spatial frequencies present at this resolution;
feeding $128 \times 128$ slices directly would shift
the effective receptive field by a factor of
$224/128 = 1.75$, misaligning DCE-MRI spatial content
with the pretrained feature space.

\paragraph{Rationale for upscaling to $224 \times 224$.}
Each $128 \times 128$ axial slice is upsampled to
$224 \times 224$ pixels before backbone processing.
This step is not redundant interpolation: the
ConvNeXt-Nano backbone was pretrained on ImageNet-12k at
$224 \times 224$ resolution and its convolutional
filters are tuned to detect edges, textures, and
semantic structures at the spatial frequencies
present at that resolution.
Upscaling to $224 \times 224$ brings the DCE-MRI
spatial frequency content into alignment with the
ImageNet-12k distribution the backbone was optimised
for, preserving the pretrained feature detectors
that encode enhancement patterns at the correct
scale.
The bilinear interpolation used for upscaling
introduces no new information; it is a geometric
alignment operation, not a super-resolution step.

Because the backbone expects a three-channel RGB input,
each single-channel greyscale slice is replicated along the
channel dimension to produce a tensor of shape
$(3, 224, 224)$.
Each slice is subsequently normalised to zero-channel mean
and unit-channel standard deviation using $\mu = 0.5$ and
$\sigma = 0.25$, values chosen empirically to centre the
DCE-MRI intensity distribution within the range that
ImageNet-pretrained networks expect.

The 2.5D approach offers two advantages over a native 3D
convolutional approach.
First, it allows the use of powerful 2D ImageNet-pretrained
backbones without the need to adapt or retrain
three-dimensional convolutional kernels from scratch.
Second, it reduces the peak VRAM requirement to levels
compatible with the 6 GB GTX~1660~Ti used in this work,
whereas a comparable 3D volume input would exceed the
available memory budget.
The spatial context across slices is recovered downstream
by the Multiple Instance Learning aggregation head, which
treats the 64 slices of a single volume as a bag of
instances and learns a weighted combination of their
features \cite{ilse2018attention}.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Data Augmentation}
\label{sec:augmentation}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Augmentation is applied during training only and operates
on the three-dimensional volume prior to slice extraction.
The following transforms are applied to the full
$64 \times 128 \times 128$ volume with the probabilities
and parameters listed below.
Random horizontal and vertical flips are applied with
probability $p=0.5$ independently.
Random rotations by multiples of $90^\circ$
($0^\circ$, $90^\circ$, $180^\circ$, $270^\circ$) are applied
uniformly at random.
Intensity scaling multiplies all pixel values by a scalar
drawn uniformly from $[0.9, 1.1]$, simulating scanner
calibration differences.
Additive Gaussian noise with standard deviation
$\sigma=0.02$ is added to the normalised slice.

Three-dimensional elastic deformation, which was initially
considered via the MONAI library, was excluded from the final
pipeline because its CPU-based implementation introduced a
latency of 30 to 60 seconds per volume at the available
hardware, making full-epoch training computationally
infeasible.

The probability $p = 0.5$ for each spatial transform
follows standard practice in medical image analysis
\cite{litjens2017survey}, ensuring the model sees
both original and augmented views with equal frequency.
Rotations are restricted to multiples of $90^\circ$
to preserve anatomical plausibility: arbitrary-angle
rotations introduce corner interpolation artefacts
incompatible with the axial acquisition geometry of
DCE-MRI.
The intensity scaling range $[0.9, 1.1]$ models the
inter-scanner calibration variability of approximately
$\pm 10\%$ reported in breast MRI reproducibility
studies \cite{lavdas2019}.
Additive Gaussian noise ($\sigma = 0.02$) corresponds
to a 2\% signal perturbation on normalised $[0,1]$
intensities, consistent with the noise floor of
clinical 1.5\,T and 3\,T DCE-MRI acquisitions.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Cross-Validation Split}
\label{sec:cv-split}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The dataset is partitioned using stratified group
five-fold cross-validation implemented via
\texttt{StratifiedGroupKFold} from scikit-learn with a
fixed random seed of 42.
The \emph{group} variable is the patient identifier, which
ensures that no patient contributes slices to both the
training and validation sets of any fold, thereby preventing
data leakage at the patient level.
The \emph{stratification} variable is the molecular subtype
label, which ensures that the class proportion in each fold
approximates the overall dataset distribution.

With 737 patients and five folds, each validation fold
contains approximately 148 patients and each training fold
approximately 589 patients.
Because HER2 is the minority class with only 46 samples,
each validation fold contains approximately nine HER2 patients,
which makes the per-fold macro F1 score for this class
sensitive to single misclassifications.
The five-fold mean and standard deviation are therefore
reported together as the primary performance estimate.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{ConvNeXt-Nano Backbone}
\label{sec:convnext-backbone}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The local model backbone is ConvNeXt-Nano
\cite{liu2022convnet}, a modern pure-convolutional
architecture whose design draws on insights from Vision
Transformers while retaining the inductive biases of
convolution.

The choice of ConvNeXt-Nano was made after evaluating
five candidate 2D backbone architectures against three
constraints imposed by the hardware budget: the peak
VRAM consumption at batch size~2 with the MIL head
must remain below 6~GB, the ImageNet pretrain quality
must maximise the feature richness available for
fine-tuning on a 737-patient cohort, and the parameter
count must be small enough for stable convergence with
the limited training data available.
The selection followed a process of elimination across
the five candidates in Table~\ref{tab:backbone-comparison}:
ViT-Small/16 and DINOv2-Small were set aside on practical
grounds (inference speed and VRAM budget respectively),
ResNet-50 lacked the pretrained accuracy needed for
reliable minority-class discrimination, and ConvNeXt-Nano

parameters are jointly optimised.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Gated Attention Multiple Instance Learning Head}
\label{sec:mil-head}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The MIL head aggregates the 64 per-slice feature vectors
produced by the backbone into a single volume-level
representation, following the gated attention mechanism of
Ilse~et~al.\ \cite{ilse2018attention}.

Let $\mathbf{f}_k \in \mathbb{R}^{640}$ denote the backbone
feature vector for the $k$-th axial slice,
$k \in \{1, \ldots, 64\}$.
Following the Gated Attention MIL design of
\textcite{ilse2018attention}, a shared projection layer
first applies a Gaussian Error Linear Unit (GELU)
activation to map each feature to a lower-dimensional
space before the attention mechanism operates on it:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{h}_k = \mathrm{GELU}(W_{\mathrm{proj}}\,\mathbf{f}_k)
  \in \mathbb{R}^{256},
  \label{eq:proj}
\end{equation}
where $W_{\mathrm{proj}} \in \mathbb{R}^{256 \times 640}$
and dropout with rate 0.3 is applied after the activation.

The projected feature is then passed through the gated
attention mechanism itself: the score for slice~$k$ is
\begin{equation}
  a_k
  = \mathbf{w}^{\top}
    \bigl(
      \tanh(V\,\mathbf{h}_k)
      \odot
      \sigma(U\,\mathbf{h}_k)
    \bigr),
  \label{eq:gated-attention}
\end{equation}
where $V, U \in \mathbb{R}^{128 \times 256}$ are learnable
weight matrices, $\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^{128}$ is a
learnable weight vector, $\odot$ denotes element-wise
multiplication, $\tanh$ controls the magnitude of the
contribution, and $\sigma$ acts as a gate that suppresses
uninformative slices.
The raw scores are normalised across the 64 slices by a
softmax:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{a}_k
  = \frac{\exp(a_k)}{\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{64} \exp(a_j)}.
  \label{eq:attn-softmax}
\end{equation}

The volume-level representation is the weighted sum of
projected features:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{z}
  = \sum_{k=1}^{64} \hat{a}_k\, \mathbf{h}_k
  \in \mathbb{R}^{256}.
  \label{eq:mil-aggregate}
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.72\linewidth]{figures/ilse2018_attention_mil.png}
  \caption[Attention-based MIL pooling]{Attention-based
    multiple instance learning pooling.
    Each instance (axial slice) produces a feature vector;
    the attention network assigns a scalar score to each
    instance; the bag-level representation is the weighted
    sum of instance features under a softmax normalisation.
    Adapted from \cite{ilse2018attention}.}
  \label{fig:mil-pooling}
\end{figure}

Finally, a linear classifier maps $\mathbf{z}$ to the
class logit vector:
$\mathbf{y} = W_{\mathrm{cls}}\,\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^{C}$,
where $C \in \{4, 2\}$ for the four-class and binary
experiments respectively.

The attention weight matrices are initialised from
$\mathcal{N}(0, 0.01)$ to prevent the attention scores from
saturating to a degenerate uniform distribution at the start
of training.
The total number of trainable parameters in the MIL head
is 231\,557.

\subsubsection*{From Formula to Code: Three Proofs}

The three operations that define Gated Attention MIL
each have an exact, verifiable counterpart in the
implementation (\texttt{model.py}).
The following code extracts confirm that the mathematical
description and the executable code are identical.

% ── CODE PROOF 1 ─────────────────────────────────────────

\paragraph{Code Proof 1 — what \texttt{Linear(256\,$\to$\,128)} does.}

The four learnable layers of the attention mechanism
are declared in the constructor of \texttt{GatedAttentionMIL}
(\texttt{model.py}, lines 29--32):

\begin{verbatim}
self.proj   = nn.Linear(in_dim,    proj_dim)   # 640 -> 256
self.attn_V = nn.Linear(proj_dim,  attn_dim)   # 256 -> 128  (content)
self.attn_U = nn.Linear(proj_dim,  attn_dim)   # 256 -> 128  (gate)
self.attn_w = nn.Linear(attn_dim,  1)          # 128 -> 1    (score)
\end{verbatim}

\texttt{nn.Linear(256, 128)} creates a weight matrix
$\mathbf{W} \in \mathbb{R}^{128 \times 256}$
and a bias vector $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^{128}$.
When called on a 256-dimensional input vector
$\mathbf{h}$, it computes:
\begin{equation}
  \text{output} = \mathbf{W}\,\mathbf{h} + \mathbf{b}
  \in \mathbb{R}^{128}.
\end{equation}
This is 128 simultaneous weighted sums, each using
all 256 inputs with its own row of weights.
The total number of learnable parameters is
$128 \times 256 + 128 = 32{,}896$ numbers, all
updated by gradient descent during training.
Nobody chose these weights manually:
they are initialised randomly and learned from the
patient-level labels through backpropagation.

% ── CODE PROOF 2 ─────────────────────────────────────────

\paragraph{Code Proof 2 — the gated attention formula.}

The formal gated attention score for slice~$i$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \text{score}_i
  = \mathbf{w}^\top
    \bigl(\tanh(\mathbf{V}\,\mathbf{h}_i)
    \;\odot\;
    \sigma(\mathbf{U}\,\mathbf{h}_i)\bigr),
  \label{eq:gated-attention-code}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{h}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{256}$ is the
projected feature of slice~$i$,
$\mathbf{V}$ and $\mathbf{U}$ are the two
$\mathbb{R}^{128 \times 256}$ weight matrices,
$\odot$ denotes element-wise multiplication, and
$\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^{128}$ collapses the
result to a single scalar.

This formula is implemented \emph{verbatim} in the
\texttt{forward} method
(\texttt{model.py}, lines 49--51):

\begin{verbatim}
v      = torch.tanh(self.attn_V(h))          # tanh(V * h)
u      = torch.sigmoid(self.attn_U(h))       # sigmoid(U * h)
scores = self.attn_w(v * u).squeeze(-1)      # w . (v elemwise* u)
\end{verbatim}

Reading line by line against Equation~(\ref{eq:gated-attention}):
\texttt{self.attn\_V(h)} computes $\mathbf{V}\,\mathbf{h}$;
\texttt{torch.tanh(...)} applies $\tanh$, producing the
\emph{content} branch $\mathbf{v}$.
\texttt{self.attn\_U(h)} computes $\mathbf{U}\,\mathbf{h}$;
\texttt{torch.sigmoid(...)} applies $\sigma$, producing
the \emph{gate} $\mathbf{u}$.
The expression \texttt{v~*~u} is the
element-wise product~$\odot$, and
\texttt{self.attn\_w(...)} applies
$\mathbf{w}^\top(\cdot)$.
The gate~$\mathbf{u}$ acts as a confidence filter:
values near~1 allow the content signal through;
values near~0 suppress it.
A slice that appears visually unusual due to an imaging
artefact may produce a high content score but a low
gate value, resulting in near-zero net attention.

% ── CODE PROOF 3 ─────────────────────────────────────────

\paragraph{Code Proof 3 — the weighted sum that builds the patient feature.}

After computing one score per slice, the scores are
converted to attention weights and used to pool 64
slice features into a single patient-level representation
(\texttt{model.py}, lines 52--53):

\begin{verbatim}
attn   = F.softmax(scores, dim=1)            # weights sum to 1
pooled = (h * attn.unsqueeze(-1)).sum(dim=1) # sum_i a_i * h_i
\end{verbatim}

\texttt{F.softmax(scores, dim=1)} converts the 64 raw
scores to 64 non-negative weights that sum to exactly~1.0.
\texttt{attn.unsqueeze(-1)} reshapes the weights from
shape $(B,\,64)$ to $(B,\,64,\,1)$ so that each scalar
weight can be broadcast across the 256 feature dimensions
of the corresponding slice.
The \texttt{.sum(dim=1)} then collapses the slice
dimension, computing:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{z} = \sum_{i=1}^{64} a_i\,\mathbf{h}_i
  \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{256},
\end{equation}
where $a_i$ is the attention weight of slice~$i$.
The result $\mathbf{z}$ is the patient-level feature
vector: a single 256-dimensional representation that
carries the most information from whichever slices
received the highest attention weights.
Since no tumour masks are available, the model learns
during training which slices deserve high weights
purely from the patient-level Luminal/Non-Luminal label.
Slices containing the tumour receive high~$a_i$ because
attending to them consistently reduces the classification
loss; empty or artefactual slices receive~$a_i \approx 0$.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Full Forward Pass}
\label{sec:forward-pass}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The full forward pass for one volume proceeds as follows.
The $64 \times 128 \times 128$ preprocessed volume is
decomposed into 64 axial slices and upsampled to
$64 \times 3 \times 224 \times 224$.
To manage VRAM, slices are processed through the backbone
in two chunks of 32, and the resulting feature tensors are
concatenated along the slice dimension.
The MIL head then computes the gated attention scores and
aggregates the features into the volume-level embedding
$\mathbf{z}$.
The classifier produces the final logit vector from
$\mathbf{z}$.
The overall architecture contains 15\,184\,117 parameters.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{figures/ch3_model_architecture.png}
  \caption[ConvNeXt-Nano + Gated Attention MIL architecture]{%
    Architecture of the ConvNeXt-Nano + Gated
    Attention MIL classifier. Each of the 64 axial slices
    is processed independently through the backbone to
    produce a 640-dimensional feature vector. The MIL head

% ============================================================
\section{Three-Stage Training Pipeline}
\label{sec:training-strategy}
% ============================================================


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Stage 1: Head Warmup}
\label{sec:stage1}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Stage~1 trains only the MIL head for 20 epochs with the
backbone parameters frozen.
The purpose of this stage is to stabilise the attention
weights before backbone fine-tuning begins: since the
backbone is already well-pretrained on ImageNet-12k, its
features are informative from the first epoch, and allowing
the attention head to reach a reasonable operating point
before joint training avoids the risk of the backbone
adapting to uninformative attention distributions.

The optimiser is AdamW \cite{loshchilov2019adamw} (Adam with
decoupled weight decay) with a learning rate of $10^{-3}$
and a weight decay of 0.05.
These values follow standard practice for fine-tuning
pretrained convolutional backbones on small medical imaging
datasets \cite{guan2024federated}.
A cosine annealing schedule with linear warmup over the
first two epochs governs the learning rate.
The loss function is cross-entropy with label smoothing
of 0.1.
Training uses an effective batch size of 16, implemented
as a physical batch of 2 with 8 gradient accumulation
steps, to remain within the 6 GB VRAM budget.
The best checkpoint by validation macro F1 is saved as
\texttt{fold\{N\}\_stage1\_best.pt}.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Stage 2: Joint Fine-tuning}
\label{sec:stage2}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Stage~2 trains all 15.18 million parameters jointly for
80 epochs.
The learning rates are set to $5 \times 10^{-5}$ for the
backbone and $10^{-3}$ for the MIL head, with weight decay
of 0.05 and 0.1 respectively, governed by independent
cosine annealing schedules.
The backbone learning rate is set one order of magnitude
below the head learning rate to prevent catastrophic
forgetting of pretrained ImageNet-12k features during
joint fine-tuning.
Dropout with rate $0.3$ (values of $0.1$ and $0.5$ were
tested in preliminary fold~0 experiments; $0.3$ produced
the highest validation F1) is applied in the MIL projection
layer.
Gradient norms are clipped to a maximum of 1.0 at each step.
The 80-epoch budget was determined empirically:
fewer than 55~epochs does not allow \textsc{ASAM}
activation; more than 80~epochs showed diminishing
returns on validation F1 in preliminary fold~0 experiments.

The choice of loss function followed a process of
elimination on fold~0: simpler alternatives were found to
collapse toward majority-class prediction under the
dataset's 10:1 imbalance.
LDAM \cite{cao2019ldam} was the first configuration to
produce non-trivial recall for HER2 and Triple-Negative,
and was retained for all subsequent experiments.

\paragraph{Loss function: LDAM.}
The Label-Distribution-Aware Margin (LDAM) loss
\cite{cao2019ldam} is used throughout all 80 epochs.
LDAM assigns a per-class margin $\Delta_y$ that is inversely
proportional to the fourth root of the class frequency,
penalising misclassification of minority classes more heavily:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta_y = \frac{C}{n_y^{1/4}},
  \label{eq:ldam-margin}
\end{equation}
where $n_y$ is the number of training samples for class~$y$
and $C$ is a scaling constant chosen so that the maximum
margin across classes equals $m_{\max}$: specifically
$C = m_{\max} \cdot (\min_y n_y)^{1/4}$.
This formula and scaling scheme follow
Cao~et~al.\ \cite{cao2019ldam}.
The modified cross-entropy loss with margin is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{LDAM}}(\mathbf{z}, y)
  = -\log
    \frac{
      \exp(z_y - \Delta_y)
    }{
      \exp(z_y - \Delta_y)
      + \displaystyle\sum_{j \neq y} \exp(z_j)
    },
  \label{eq:ldam-loss}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^C$ is the logit vector.
The hyperparameters are set to a maximum margin
$m_{\max} = 0.2$, scale $s = 15$, and $\beta = 0.9999$.
These values are adopted from the recommended configuration
of \textcite{cao2019ldam} for long-tailed recognition and
confirmed on fold~0 validation prior to full five-fold
training.
Switching from LDAM to class-balanced cross-entropy at an
intermediate epoch was tested in preliminary experiments but
consistently caused class-prediction collapse; LDAM was
therefore retained for all 80 epochs without schedule change.
Figure~\ref{fig:ldam-margin} illustrates the
class-frequency margin relationship.

Figure~\ref{fig:ldam-margin} shows how the per-class margin
$\Delta_y$ decreases as class frequency $n_y$ increases,
assigning the largest margin to HER2-enriched (46 samples)
and the smallest to Luminal~A (481 samples).

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.76\linewidth]{figures/cao2019_ldam_margin.png}
  \caption[Label-distribution-aware margin loss]{%
    Label-distribution-aware margin loss. Minority
    classes receive larger decision margins $\Delta_y$
    inversely proportional to $n_y^{1/4}$, pushing the
    classifier to allocate more representation capacity
    to rare subtypes such as HER2-enriched.
    Adapted from \cite{cao2019ldam}.}
  \label{fig:ldam-margin}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Exponential Moving Average.}
An exponential moving average (EMA) of the model weights
is maintained throughout Stage~2 with a decay of 0.99.
Validation metrics are computed on the EMA model rather
than the live model, which provides smoother and more
reliable estimates of generalisation performance.

After Stage~2 with LDAM alone, validation performance on
fold~0 was meaningful but unstable across seeds.
Reading the ASAM paper \cite{kwon2021asam}, the
flat-minimum argument matched the observed instability:
the model appeared to be landing in sharp basins that
generalised poorly to the held-out validation fold.
ASAM was added at epoch~55 following the paper's
recommendation to activate it once the model is near a
local minimum.

\paragraph{ASAM.}
Adaptive Sharpness-Aware Minimization (ASAM)
\cite{kwon2021asam} is activated from epoch 55 onward.
ASAM perturbs the model parameters in the direction of
maximum loss increase within an adaptive neighbourhood of
radius $\rho = 0.05$, then takes a gradient step at the
perturbed point to steer the optimisation toward flatter
loss basins.
Each ASAM step requires two forward passes, doubling the
per-step compute from epoch 55 to the end of Stage~2.
\textsc{ASAM} is activated at epoch~55, the final third
of Stage~2, following the recommendation of
\textcite{kwon2021asam} to apply sharpness-aware perturbation
once the model approaches a local minimum.
At epoch~55 the cosine learning rate schedule has decayed
to approximately 15\,\% of its peak value, indicating that
large-step gradient exploration is complete.
All Stage~2 training is conducted in single-precision
floating point (FP32) to avoid numerical instability in
two computations critical to correctness: the \textsc{ASAM}
perturbation norm and the \textsc{LDAM} margin calculation,
both of which involve ratios of small class frequencies that
risk underflow in half-precision arithmetic.

\paragraph{SWA.}
Stochastic Weight Averaging \cite{izmailov2018swa}
is activated at epoch~65, corresponding to 81\,\% of the
Stage~2 training budget.
\textcite{izmailov2018swa} recommend initiating checkpoint
averaging at approximately 75\,\% of the training
budget once the learning rate has decayed to a low
value; epoch~65 satisfies this criterion, with the
cosine schedule having reached approximately 8\,\% of
its peak learning rate.
The 16-epoch averaging window (epochs~65--80) provides
sufficient checkpoints for the geometric ensemble to
stabilise.
This schedule follows the \textsc{ASAM} activation
period (epochs~55--64): \textsc{ASAM} drives the
optimisation toward a flat minimum, and \textsc{SWA}
then averages checkpoints within that region, further
improving generalisation \cite{izmailov2018swa}.
A minimum of 10 averaging epochs is recommended by
\textcite{izmailov2018swa}; 16~epochs satisfies this
requirement with a margin.

The best Stage~2 checkpoint by validation macro F1 is saved
as \texttt{fold\{N\}\_stage2\_best.pt}.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/ch3_training_stages.png}
  \caption[Three-stage training pipeline]{Three-stage
    training pipeline. Stage~1 warms up
    the MIL head for 20 epochs with the backbone frozen.
    Stage~2 jointly fine-tunes all 15.18M parameters for
    80 epochs using LDAM loss, with ASAM activated at

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Stage 3: Classifier Retraining (cRT)}
\label{sec:stage3}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Stage~3 addresses a known failure mode of imbalanced
training: even with LDAM and class-balanced sampling, the
classifier head may become miscalibrated toward the majority
class because its gradients are dominated by the Luminal~A
examples during backbone fine-tuning.
The cRT (Classifier Retraining) strategy fixes this by
freezing the backbone after Stage~2 and retraining only the
final linear classifier with perfectly balanced class
sampling.

Even after Stage~2, a systematic observation remained:
the classifier over-predicted Luminal~A in every fold.
Reading Kang~et~al.\ \cite{kang2020decoupling}, who showed
that this imbalanced-head phenomenon is separable from
representation quality, the cRT stage was adopted directly
from their protocol: freeze the backbone, reset the head,
and retrain with a class-balanced sampler.

The Stage~2 best checkpoint is loaded, the backbone is frozen,
and the 256-dimensional MIL embeddings for all training
volumes are cached.
The cRT stage trains for 300 epochs with
AdamW ($\eta = 10^{-3}$, weight decay $= 0.01$).
The learning rate is set substantially above the
Stage~2 backbone value ($5 \times 10^{-5}$) because
the head is randomly re-initialised and requires larger
initial gradient steps to converge; a converged
pretrained head would require a much smaller rate to
avoid catastrophic forgetting.
Weight decay of $0.01$ provides $\ell_2$ regularisation
appropriate for the small parameter count
($256 \times 2 + 2 = 514$ parameters) relative to the
589-patient training set.
The 300-epoch budget ensures convergence across all
$J = 10$ random seeds \cite{kang2020decoupling}:
with a class-balanced sampler at batch size~32 on
589 patients, one epoch comprises approximately
$\lceil 589/32 \rceil = 19$ gradient steps;
300 epochs (${\approx}5{,}700$ steps) is necessary
for a randomly initialised Linear$(256 \to 2)$ head
to reach its optimum.
\textcite{kang2020decoupling} use 10 epochs for cRT on
ImageNet-LT where millions of samples yield
${\gg}1{,}000$ steps per epoch; the substantially
smaller dataset requires a proportionally larger epoch
count to accumulate a comparable number of gradient
steps.
Seed selection by validation macro F1 acts as an
implicit early stopping criterion robust to random
initialisation, and the best checkpoint is saved as
\texttt{fold\{N\}\_stage3\_best.pt}.

\section{Federated Learning Configuration and Baselines}
\label{sec:fl-setup}
% ============================================================

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Binary Reformulation for Federated Training}
\label{sec:binary-task}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The four-class molecular subtype classification task
presents a fundamental data scarcity constraint that
motivates a clinically grounded reformulation.
As established in Section~\ref{sec:cv-split}, the HER2
class contains only 46 patients across all 737 samples,
yielding approximately nine HER2 validation patients per
fold under five-fold cross-validation.
At this scale, a single misclassified HER2 patient shifts
the per-class F1 score by approximately 0.08 points,
making the four-class validation metric statistically
unstable and setting a practical performance ceiling on
the centralised experiment of approximately $0.40$ macro F1.

The four-class problem is therefore reformulated as a
binary classification task for the federated learning
experiment.
Luminal~A and Luminal~B subtypes are merged into a single
\emph{Luminal} class (label~0, $n=557$), and HER2-enriched
and Triple-Negative subtypes are merged into a single
\emph{Non-Luminal} class (label~1, $n=180$).

This reformulation is doubly motivated.
Clinically, the primary decision boundary in breast
oncology treatment planning is hormone receptor status:
Luminal tumours (ER/PR-positive) are candidates for
endocrine therapy such as tamoxifen or aromatase
inhibitors, while Non-Luminal tumours are not and are
instead treated with cytotoxic chemotherapy and, where
applicable, anti-HER2 agents \cite{alves2023breastmri}.
The binary task therefore maps directly to the clinical
triage decision of hormone therapy eligibility, making
the classification output immediately actionable in a
privacy-constrained federated setting.

Methodologically, the binary reformulation is an
optimisation decision that directly reduces Problem~1
severity: the imbalance ratio drops from 10.4:1
(Luminal~A versus HER2 in the four-class setting) to
3.1:1 (Luminal versus Non-Luminal), tripling the
effective minority class size from 46 to 180 samples.
This makes the SCAFFOLD convergence behaviour
measurable within the 20-round budget and the LDAM
margin calibration stable across folds.
The resulting binary partition across the three
simulated hospitals is reported in
Section~\ref{sec:noniid-partition}.
Without this reformulation, the HER2 per-client sample
counts under any realistic Dirichlet partition would drop to
single digits at the smaller clients, making convergence
monitoring statistically meaningless.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Federated Client Configuration}
\label{sec:fl-clients}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The binary-labelled dataset is partitioned into three
simulated hospital clients using the Dirichlet strategy
described in Section~\ref{sec:noniid-partition} with
$\alpha = 0.5$ (primary experiment).
Each client runs the same local training pipeline as the
centralised experiment (Stages~1 through~3), operating
exclusively on its local partition.
At the end of each local training round, the client
serialises its model parameters and transmits them to the
Flower coordination server.
No raw imaging data, patient identifiers, or label
information are transmitted.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Choice of Aggregation Strategies}
\label{sec:agg-choice}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The experimental comparison uses FedAvg as the
baseline and SCAFFOLD as the optimised variant.
This choice is not arbitrary; it follows from an
analysis of which algorithm best addresses the
specific bottleneck of the three-hospital
non-IID breast MRI partition.

\textbf{Why FedAvg as baseline.}
FedAvg \cite{mcmahan2017fedavg} is the reference
algorithm for every FL publication: omitting it would
make the results incomparable to the existing
literature.
It also represents the naive application of FL without
any correction for heterogeneity, and its performance
gap relative to the centralised baseline is precisely
the quantity this thesis seeks to minimise.

\textbf{Why SCAFFOLD and not FedProx or FedNova.}
The primary failure mode of FedAvg on non-IID data is
\emph{client drift}: local models are pulled toward
their own local optima and the aggregated average is a
poor approximation of the global optimum
\cite{karimireddy2020scaffold}.
FedProx \cite{li2020fedprox} addresses drift
implicitly by penalising deviation from the global
model, but its convergence guarantee only reaches a
\emph{neighbourhood} of the optimum whose size grows
with the heterogeneity parameter $\mu$.
FedNova \cite{li2020fednova} addresses objective
inconsistency from variable local steps, but in this
setting all clients perform exactly $E=2$ local epochs,
so this inconsistency is absent by construction.
SCAFFOLD \cite{karimireddy2020scaffold} eliminates
client drift \emph{exactly} using control variates and
is the only algorithm with a convergence guarantee
matching centralised SGD regardless of heterogeneity
degree.
Table~\ref{tab:agg-choice} summarises this reasoning.

The empirical partition reinforces this choice:
Hospitals~1 and~2 together hold only 10 of the
180 Non-Luminal training patients, meaning their
local gradient signals for this class are based
on fewer than 5 examples each.
Under FedAvg, these two clients would contribute
Luminal-biased updates that suppress the global
model's Non-Luminal recognition, regardless of
the accurate Non-Luminal gradients from Hospital~3.
SCAFFOLD's per-client control variates correct
precisely this systematic bias.

\subsection{FedAvg Baseline}
\label{sec:fedavg-baseline}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

FedAvg \cite{mcmahan2017fedavg} is the reference
aggregation strategy against which all optimised
variants are compared.
It represents the unoptimised federated baseline:
no correction for client drift (Problem~2) and no
server-side awareness of client class imbalance
(Problem~1).
Its performance therefore defines the lower bound
of the optimisation space; the gap between FedAvg
and SCAFFOLD quantifies the contribution of drift
correction alone, and the gap between SCAFFOLD and
the centralised baseline quantifies the residual
optimisation headroom that the FedSCRT stage
(Section~\ref{sec:fl-optimised}) is designed to
close.

At each communication round $t$, all three clients are
selected ($C = 1.0$, full participation).
Each client performs $E$ local training epochs on its
local partition, then uploads its parameter vector
$\mathbf{w}_k^{(t+1)}$.
The server then computes the sample-weighted aggregate
exactly as defined in the original FedAvg formulation
of \textcite{mcmahan2017fedavg}:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{w}^{(t+1)}
  = \sum_{k=1}^{3}
    \frac{n_k}{\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{3} n_j}\;
    \mathbf{w}_k^{(t+1)},
  \label{eq:fedavg-ch3}
\end{equation}
where $n_k$ is the number of binary-labelled training
samples held by client $k$.
The global model $\mathbf{w}^{(t+1)}$ is then broadcast
to all clients as the initialisation for round $t+1$.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Optimised FL Variant}
\label{sec:fl-optimised}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Four optimisation variants are evaluated against the
FedAvg baseline, yielding six experimental runs in total.

\textbf{Run~1 --- FedAvg, $\alpha=0.5$} (primary baseline):
Standard weighted averaging with no drift correction.
Defines the unoptimised lower bound.

\textbf{Run~2 --- Momentum-FedAvg, $\alpha=0.5$}:
This variant transplants the server-side momentum idea
from classical optimisation \cite{kingma2015adam} onto
the FedAvg aggregation step itself ($\beta = 0.9$),
on the hypothesis that smoothing the aggregated update
direction across rounds would reduce the oscillation
that non-IID drift introduces.
The server update becomes:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{v}^{(t+1)} = \beta\,\mathbf{v}^{(t)}
    + \Delta\mathbf{w}^{(t)}, \quad
  \mathbf{w}^{(t+1)} = \mathbf{w}^{(t)}
    + \eta_s\,\mathbf{v}^{(t+1)},
  \label{eq:momentum-fedavg}
\end{equation}
where $\eta_s=1.0$ is the server learning rate.
Momentum smooths oscillations in the server update
direction that are amplified by non-IID client drift,
improving effective use of the 20-round budget.

\textbf{Run~3 --- SCAFFOLD, $\alpha=0.5$} (primary contribution):
Control-variate drift correction as described in
Section~\ref{sec:scaffold}.
Directly addresses client drift caused by
Hospital~3's reversed dominant class.

\textbf{Run~4 --- SCAFFOLD + FedSCRT, $\alpha=0.5$}
(federated classifier retraining):
After $R=20$ SCAFFOLD rounds, the global backbone
is frozen and each hospital retrains a balanced
classifier head locally (300 epochs, 10 seeds).
The three heads are aggregated by the Flower server
via weighted FedAvg.
This stage addresses the head miscalibration that
persists after SCAFFOLD convergence, extending the
Classifier Retraining technique of
\cite{cao2019ldam} to the federated setting.

\subsubsection*{FedSCRT: Formal Description}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:fedscrt} presents the complete
FedSCRT procedure.
The algorithm operates in two sequential phases.
Phase~1 runs standard SCAFFOLD aggregation for $R$
communication rounds to produce a globally consistent
backbone $\Psi^*_\theta$.
Phase~2 decouples classifier calibration from backbone
learning: each hospital independently retrains a fresh
classification head on its local data using class-balanced
sampling, and the server aggregates the resulting heads
via weighted averaging.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{FedSCRT: Federated Staged Classifier Retraining}
\label{alg:fedscrt}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require $K$ clients with local datasets $\{\mathcal{D}_k\}_{k=1}^K$,
         $R$ communication rounds,
         $E$ local epochs per round,
         $T$ cRT epochs per seed,
         $J$ independent seeds,
         samples per class $m$,
         validation sets $\{\mathcal{V}_k\}_{k=1}^K$
\Ensure  Global model $(\Psi^*_\theta,\, \mathbf{w}^*)$

\Statex
\Statex \textbf{Phase 1 --- SCAFFOLD Backbone Training}
\Statex \hrulefill

\State Initialise $\mathbf{W}^{(0)}$,\;
       $\mathbf{c} \leftarrow \mathbf{0}$,\;
       $\mathbf{c}_k \leftarrow \mathbf{0}\ \forall k$

\For{$t = 0,\, 1,\, \ldots,\, R-1$}
  \State Server broadcasts $(\mathbf{W}^{(t)},\, \mathbf{c})$
         to all $K$ clients

  \For{each client $k \in \{1,\ldots,K\}$
       \textbf{in parallel}}
    \State $\mathbf{w}_k \leftarrow \mathbf{W}^{(t)}$
    \For{$e = 1$ \textbf{to} $E$}
      \State Sample mini-batch
             $\mathcal{B}_k \sim \mathcal{D}_k$
      \State $\mathbf{w}_k \leftarrow \mathbf{w}_k
             - \eta\!\left(
               \nabla F_k(\mathbf{w}_k;\,\mathcal{B}_k)
               - \mathbf{c}_k + \mathbf{c}
             \right)$
             \Comment{drift-corrected local step}
    \EndFor
    \State $\Delta\mathbf{c}_k \leftarrow
           \dfrac{1}{E\eta}
           \!\left(\mathbf{W}^{(t)} - \mathbf{w}_k\right)
           - \mathbf{c}$
           \Comment{variate correction (eq.~\ref{eq:scaffold-variate})}
    \State Upload $\bigl(\mathbf{w}_k,\;
           \Delta\mathbf{c}_k\bigr)$ to server
  \EndFor

  \State $\mathbf{W}^{(t+1)} \leftarrow
         \displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^K
         \frac{n_k}{n}\,\mathbf{w}_k$
         \Comment{weighted model aggregation}
  \State $\mathbf{c} \leftarrow
         \mathbf{c} +
         \dfrac{1}{K}
         \displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^K
         \Delta\mathbf{c}_k$
         \Comment{global variate update}
\EndFor

\State $\Psi^*_\theta \leftarrow
       \operatorname{backbone}\!\left(\mathbf{W}^{(R)}\right)$
       \Comment{freeze; discard head weights}

\Statex
\Statex \textbf{Phase 2 --- Federated Classifier Retraining}
\Statex \hrulefill

\For{each client $k \in \{1,\ldots,K\}$
     \textbf{in parallel}}

  \State $\mathbf{z}_i \leftarrow \Psi^*_\theta(x_i)\;
         \forall x_i \in \mathcal{D}_k$
         \Comment{cache embeddings, no gradient}

  \For{$j = 1$ \textbf{to} $J$}
    \State Initialise fresh head $\mathbf{w}_{k,j}$
    \For{$\tau = 1$ \textbf{to} $T$}
      \State $\mathcal{B}_k^{\mathrm{bal}} \leftarrow$
             draw $m$ samples per class from $\mathcal{D}_k$
             \Comment{Section~\ref{sec:fedscrt-theory}}
      \State $\mathbf{w}_{k,j} \leftarrow
             \mathbf{w}_{k,j}
             - \eta_h\,
             \nabla_{\!\mathbf{w}}\,
             \mathcal{L}_k^{\mathrm{bal}}
             \!\left(\mathbf{w}_{k,j};\;
             \mathcal{B}_k^{\mathrm{bal}},\;
             \Psi^*_\theta\right)$
             \Comment{balanced head update}
    \EndFor
    \State $\mathrm{F1}_{k,j} \leftarrow
           \mathrm{MacroF1}
           \!\left(\mathbf{w}_{k,j},\,\mathcal{V}_k\right)$
  \EndFor

  \State $\mathbf{w}_k^* \leftarrow
         \mathbf{w}_{k,\,\arg\max_{j}\,\mathrm{F1}_{k,j}}$
         \Comment{best seed by val macro F1}
  \State Upload $\mathbf{w}_k^*$ to server
\EndFor

\State $\mathbf{w}^* \leftarrow
       \displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^K
       \frac{n_k}{n}\,\mathbf{w}_k^*$
       \Comment{Section~\ref{sec:fedscrt-theory}}

\State \Return $\bigl(\Psi^*_\theta,\; \mathbf{w}^*\bigr)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Phase 1 --- SCAFFOLD Backbone Training (lines 1--15).}
The first phase is standard SCAFFOLD
\cite{karimireddy2020scaffold}.
Each client performs $E$ drift-corrected local gradient
steps per round, subtracting the client control variate
$\mathbf{c}_k$ and adding the server variate $\mathbf{c}$
to eliminate gradient bias.
After $R = 20$ rounds, the global model
$\mathbf{W}^{(R)}$ contains a backbone
$\Psi^*_\theta$ that has learned discriminative
representations from all three hospital distributions
simultaneously.
The backbone is then frozen: no further gradient updates
are applied to $\Psi^*_\theta$ in Phase~2.
This freezing is the architectural decision that makes
Section~\ref{sec:fedscrt-theory} applicable: the balanced head
gradient is unbiased only when the backbone is fixed,
because a trainable backbone would re-introduce the
imbalanced class-prior into the representation space.

\paragraph{Phase 2 --- Federated Classifier Retraining
(lines 16--27).}
Each client independently retrains the classification
head from scratch using class-balanced sampling on
its cached backbone embeddings.
The class-balanced sampler draws exactly $m$ samples
per class per mini-batch, regardless of the local
class distribution $\mathcal{P}_k(c)$.
By Section~\ref{sec:fedscrt-theory}, this renders the local
head gradient an unbiased estimator of the global
balanced objective $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bal}}$,
eliminating the influence of the local class prior.
Ten independent seeds are trained and the best by
validation macro F1 is selected, providing robustness
to random initialisation.

The server aggregates the three local heads by
weighted average (line~26), producing a global head
$\mathbf{w}^*$ that benefits from the combined
balanced gradient signal across all hospitals.
By Section~\ref{sec:fedscrt-theory}, this aggregated
head converges to the minimiser of
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bal}}$ as $T \to \infty$,
independently of how extreme the non-IID partition
is or how severe the local class imbalance.

\paragraph{Computational cost.}
Phase~1 requires $R \times K \times E$ client-epochs
of full-model gradient computation.
Phase~2 requires $K \times J \times T$ head-training
epochs on \emph{cached} embeddings: since $\Psi^*_\theta$
is frozen, each client performs a single forward pass
through the backbone ($K$ passes total) and all subsequent
training operates on pre-computed feature vectors.
The head contains $d \times C$ parameters, where
$d = 256$ (projected MIL dimension) and $C \in \{2, 4\}$,
making Phase~2 computationally negligible relative
to Phase~1.
The Phase~2 communication payload per client is exactly
$d \times C$ scalar values, three to four orders of
magnitude smaller than the backbone parameter count
transmitted in each SCAFFOLD round.

\paragraph{Privacy.}
FedSCRT transmits only model parameters at every stage.
Raw imaging data, patient identifiers, and label
distributions never leave the client institution,
consistent with the federated privacy model established
in Chapter~2.

\textbf{Runs~5--6 --- FedAvg and SCAFFOLD, $\alpha=100$}:
Near-IID comparison partitions in which all three
hospitals share the same dominant class
(Table~\ref{tab:partition-noniid}).
Performance under near-IID conditions quantifies
how much of the SCAFFOLD gain is attributable to
statistical heterogeneity versus other factors.

\subsection{Why FedSCRT Works: The Balanced Gradient Argument}
\label{sec:fedscrt-theory}

\paragraph{The problem with standard federated head training.}
When a client trains its classification head on its local
data without any adjustment, the gradient it computes is
dominated by the locally abundant class.
At Hospital~2, where 84\,\% of patients are Luminal,
the correction signal that flows into the head weights is
overwhelmingly ``predict Luminal.''
The head learns to take the lazy shortcut: predict the
majority class regardless of the scan.
This is the cause of the F1\,=\,0.43 collapse observed
in FedAvg (Section~\ref{sec:fedavg-results}).

\paragraph{How balanced sampling corrects this.}
FedSCRT idea adapted from CReFF \cite{Shang2022CReFF} and it replaces the natural sampling at each client                                                           
with \emph{class-balanced sampling}: for every training
batch, an equal number of Luminal and Non-Luminal patients
is drawn, regardless of how many of each class the hospital
actually holds.
The effect is direct.
In a naturally sampled batch at Hospital~2, Luminal
patients contribute 84\,\% of the gradient and Non-Luminal
patients contribute only 16\,\%.
In a balanced batch, both classes contribute 50\,\% each,
not because data was shared or moved, but because the
sampler physically forces equal representation in every
batch.
The local class frequency of Hospital~2 stops influencing
the gradient direction.

Crucially, this local operation at each hospital
independently produces the same effect: every hospital's
balanced gradient points toward a classifier that treats
both classes equally.
This is because the two cancer subtypes are biological
entities whose backbone features are consistent across
hospitals~\cite{Shang2022CReFF}.

To make this concrete: consider a Non-Luminal tumour
scanned at a hospital in Algiers and another Non-Luminal
tumour scanned at a hospital in Oran.
These are different patients on different machines.
Their raw pixel images look different: different
brightness, different noise, different contrast.
But after passing through the same frozen backbone
$\Psi_\theta$, both produce a 256-number vector that
lands in the same cluster of the feature space, because
the backbone responds to the tumour's enhancement
kinetics, not to the scanner's settings.
Molecular subtype is determined by the tumour's own
biology, not the machine that imaged it.
The hospitals never compared files.
The shared biology and the shared backbone provided the
alignment silently.

\paragraph{The two key lines in code.}
The entire argument above reduces to two blocks
in \texttt{fl\_train.py}:

\begin{verbatim}
# line 558 -- removes local class bias from the gradient
weights = 1.0 / counts[labels]          # w_i = 1/n_c
sampler = WeightedRandomSampler(         # draws each class equally
              weights, len(weights),
              replacement=True)

# lines 604-606 -- aggregates the three corrected heads
total      = sum(client_sizes)           # total patients across hospitals
weights    = [s / total                  # each hospital's share
              for s in client_sizes]
agg_weight = sum(w * h for w, h          # weighted average of heads
              in zip(weights, client_heads))
\end{verbatim}

The first block is the mechanism: assigning each sample
weight \texttt{1/n\_c} forces the sampler to draw each
class equally often, physically removing the local class
bias from every gradient computation.
The second block is the aggregation: because every
hospital solved the same balanced problem and their heads
converged to the same point, their weighted average is
also that same point.

\paragraph{Empirical confirmation.}
To verify that balanced sampling aligns client gradients
in practice, three synthetic clients were constructed with
class priors of 90\,\%, 50\,\%, and 10\,\% Luminal.
For each client, the head gradient was computed under
class-balanced and natural sampling, and its cosine
similarity to the global balanced gradient was measured,
averaged over 100 mini-batch draws.

Table~\ref{tab:theorem-verification} reports the results.
Under balanced sampling, all three clients produced
gradients aligned with the global target at cosine
$\geq 0.993$, regardless of their extreme prior
differences.
Under natural sampling, alignment collapsed to values
between 0.0002 and 0.281.
The 50\,\%-Luminal client achieved the highest natural
alignment because its prior was already close to balanced;
the two extreme clients were nearly orthogonal to the
global target.
This ordering confirms that the misalignment is caused
by the local class prior and that balanced sampling
eliminates it.

\paragraph{Scope and future formalisation.}
The argument above is stated informally and supported
empirically.
A formal proof of gradient unbiasedness under
class-balanced sampling in the federated setting,
including a full convergence rate analysis for the
weighted aggregation step, is identified as a direction
for future theoretical work (Section~\ref{sec:future-work}).
The empirical evidence in Table~\ref{tab:theorem-verification}
provides a sufficient foundation for the practical
conclusions drawn in this thesis.

\subsection{Primary Metric: Macro F1-Score}
\label{sec:macro-f1}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Surveys of deep learning in medical imaging recommend
class-balanced metrics over raw accuracy whenever the
diagnostic categories are imbalanced \cite{litjens2017survey},
since accuracy alone can be dominated by the majority class.
Following that recommendation, the primary evaluation metric
adopted here is the macro-averaged F1-score, the textbook
unweighted arithmetic mean of the per-class F1-scores:
\begin{equation}
  F1_{\mathrm{macro}}
  = \frac{1}{C}
    \sum_{c=1}^{C}
    \frac{2 \cdot P_c \cdot R_c}{P_c + R_c},
  \label{eq:macro-f1}
\end{equation}
where $P_c$ and $R_c$ are the precision and recall for
class $c$ respectively.
The macro average is used rather than the weighted average
because it treats all classes equally regardless of their
sample count, which appropriately penalises models that
perform well on Luminal~A but fail on HER2 or
Triple-Negative.
All validation metrics are computed on the EMA model
checkpoint with the highest validation macro F1 across
training epochs.

Table~\ref{tab:metric-choice} compares candidate
evaluation metrics for the imbalanced four-class
setting of this study.

\subsection{Secondary Metrics}
\label{sec:secondary-metrics}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

In addition to macro F1, the following metrics are reported
for both the centralised and federated experiments.
Overall classification accuracy provides a complementary
view of performance but is de-emphasised due to its
sensitivity to class imbalance.
Per-class precision, recall, and F1 are reported to diagnose
which subtypes benefit or suffer from the federated training
regime relative to the centralised baseline.
The area under the receiver operating characteristic curve
(AUC-ROC) is computed per class in a one-versus-rest fashion
and macro-averaged, providing a threshold-independent
performance measure.
Confusion matrices are reported for each fold and for the
five-fold ensemble to visualise systematic confusion patterns
between subtypes.

\subsection{Centralised vs.\ Federated Comparison}
\label{sec:fed-vs-central}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The centralised performance is estimated by the five-fold
cross-validated macro F1 of the full pipeline including
Stage~3 cRT.
The federated performance is measured by the global model's
validation macro F1 evaluated at each communication round,
averaged across the three client validation sets.
The federated performance gap (the difference between
centralised and federated macro F1) is the primary
optimisation target of this work.
Reducing this gap through the combined action of SCAFFOLD
aggregation and federated classifier retraining is the
central experimental contribution reported in Chapter~4.

Test-time augmentation (TTA) is applied at inference by
averaging predictions over four augmented views of each
slice (original, horizontal flip, vertical flip, and
horizontal-plus-vertical flip) and is applied consistently
across both the centralised and federated evaluations
to ensure comparability.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:ch3-conclusion}
% ============================================================

This chapter has described a complete experimental
methodology for federated breast MRI molecular subtype
classification.
The proposed system combines a ConvNeXt-Nano backbone with
a Gated Attention MIL head, trained through a three-stage
optimisation strategy that addresses the dataset's severe
class imbalance through LDAM loss, cRT classifier
retraining, and ASAM and SWA regularisation.
The federated experiment partitions the dataset across three
simulated hospitals using a Dirichlet non-IID strategy and
reformulates the task as binary classification to align with
the clinical hormone therapy eligibility decision boundary.

The centralised experiment provides the performance upper
bound and validates the local model architecture.
The federated experiment evaluates the cost of distributing
training across privacy-preserving silos.
Chapter~4 presents the implementation details, training
logs, and the quantitative results of both experiments.
