results of the two studies described in Chapter~3.

environment.

of the federated simulation.

Training runs were launched via PowerShell using the command:
\begin{verbatim}
python -u main.py --arch convnext_mil --stage all --fold N
    | Tee-Object -FilePath logs/foldN.log
\end{verbatim}
which simultaneously displays and persists training metrics.

after the first model download to prevent unintended network
requests.

\texttt{StratifiedGroupKFold} split and via per-seed
initialisation in the Stage~3 cRT loop to ensure
reproducibility.

\section{Federated System Configuration}
\label{sec:fed-system-setup}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Simulation of Federated Clients}
\label{sec:client-simulation}

The federated experiment is conducted in Flower's simulation
mode, which executes all three client processes sequentially
on the single available GPU rather than in parallel across
network nodes.
Single-machine simulation is the universal methodology
in algorithmic federated learning research.
The three foundational algorithms benchmarked in this
thesis were each validated through simulated client
processes on a single machine: McMahan~et~al.\ executed
all FedAvg convergence experiments by simulating up to
100 clients sequentially \cite{mcmahan2017fedavg};
Karimireddy~et~al.\ validated SCAFFOLD on synthetic
non-IID partitions in centralised simulation
\cite{karimireddy2020scaffold}; and Li~et~al.\ evaluated
FedProx under simulated system heterogeneity on a single
machine \cite{li2020fedprox}.
Single-machine simulation isolates algorithmic convergence
behaviour from network engineering variables (latency,
bandwidth, dropout), which are orthogonal to the research
questions addressed here \cite{beutel2022flower}.
Sequential execution introduces no algorithmic difference
relative to true parallel deployment: each client receives
an identical copy of the global model, trains independently
on its local partition, and returns its parameter vector
before the next client begins.

\subsection{Communication Round Configuration}
\label{sec:round-config}

The federated training runs for $R = 20$ communication
rounds, which is sufficient to observe convergence in
cross-silo settings with small cohorts
\cite{sheller2020federated}.
Each selected client performs $E = 2$ local training epochs
per round before uploading its parameters.
All three clients participate in every round ($C = 1.0$,
full participation), consistent with the persistent
availability assumption of cross-silo federation.
The total local compute per round is therefore
$K \times E = 6$ client-epochs, and the aggregate local
compute over the full experiment is $R \times K \times E
= 120$ client-epochs.

\subsection{Four-Class Performance Ceiling}
\label{sec:four-class-ceiling}

The ensemble macro F1 of $0.342$ (95\% CI: $[0.301,
0.382]$) represents the performance ceiling achievable
by a single-institution centralised system on this
dataset.
The HER2 F1 of $0.20$ reflects the fundamental constraint
of 46 training samples in the minority class: no
combination of loss reweighting, attention mechanisms,
or regularisation can substitute for sufficient training
data at this scale.
This ceiling motivates the binary reformulation
(Section~\ref{sec:binary-task}), which merges the two
rarest classes to produce a more tractable 3.1:1 imbalance
ratio, and the federated experiment, which simulates the
multi-institutional data pooling that would otherwise be
required to address this scarcity.
The macro AUC-ROC of $0.601$ confirms that the model
has learned meaningful discriminative information beyond
random chance ($0.50$), particularly for Luminal~A and
Triple-Negative subtypes where the class-specific AUCs
are substantially above the macro average.

\subsection{Binary Centralised Baseline}
\label{sec:binary-baseline}

The centralised binary baseline achieves a macro F1 of
\textbf{0.6673} (AUC-ROC = 0.6740, accuracy = 77.7\%,
Table~\ref{tab:fl-results}) on the 148 held-out fold~0
validation patients, establishing the performance ceiling
against which all federated configurations are evaluated.

The confusion matrix reveals an asymmetric classification
pattern characteristic of the residual 3.0:1 class imbalance
in the validation fold: Luminal recall reaches 90.1\%
(100 of 111 patients correctly classified) while Non-Luminal
recall stands at 40.5\% (15 of 37 patients correctly
classified).
This asymmetry reflects the limits of LDAM margin calibration
under structural data scarcity: the loss penalises Non-Luminal
errors more heavily during training, but the 37-patient
validation fold provides insufficient evidence to fully
stabilise the decision boundary.
The AUC-ROC of 0.6740 confirms discrimination beyond
chance across all decision thresholds.

This result defines the optimisation target for the
federated experiment.
A distributed system trained across three non-IID hospital
clients must approach a macro F1 of 0.6673 to minimise the
federated performance gap established in
Section~\ref{sec:fed-vs-central}.

\subsection{FedAvg Results}
\label{sec:fedavg-results}

FedAvg with $\alpha=0.5$ collapses to predicting
Luminal for every patient across all 20 rounds
(macro~F1~= 0.429, AUC~= 0.565).
The confusion matrix confirms this: the Non-Luminal
column is entirely zero.

The reason is straightforward.
Hospitals~2 and~3 together hold 509 of the 589
training patients, both strongly Luminal-dominant.
Their gradient updates dominate the weighted average,
and Hospital~1's Non-Luminal signal (57 of 80 patients)
is simply too small to counteract them.
This is the classic FedAvg failure under statistical
heterogeneity, and it appears here even though the
training set as a whole is 3.2:1 Luminal --- not
extreme by standard measures.
The performance gap relative to the centralised
baseline is 0.239~F1 points.

\subsection{Momentum-FedAvg Results}
\label{sec:momentum-results}

Momentum-FedAvg with server momentum $\beta = 0.9$
produces the most volatile AUC trajectory of all
evaluated strategies, achieving the highest peak AUC
among standard FL algorithms while collapsing to the
lowest final AUC.

The round-by-round trajectory reveals a characteristic
oscillation pattern.
The model reaches AUC-ROC = 0.645 at communication
round~6, surpassing both FedAvg (peak 0.602 at
round~19) and SCAFFOLD (peak 0.601 at round~2),
before degrading to AUC = 0.473 at round~7.
A second partial recovery to AUC = 0.630 appears at
round~18, followed by a terminal collapse to
AUC = 0.422 at round~20.
The macro F1 remains constant at 0.4286 throughout
all 20 rounds, confirming that the oscillating AUC
reflects unstable internal representations without
crossing the majority-class decision boundary.

The mechanism is the interaction between server-side
momentum and the biased majority-class gradient.
The momentum velocity vector $\mathbf{v}^{(t)}$
accumulates the weighted sum of client updates,
dominated by Hospitals~2 and~3 (combined 84\% of the
training partition) and their Luminal-biased gradients.
When momentum temporarily amplifies a corrective
Non-Luminal signal from Hospital~1, the AUC spikes;
when the accumulated majority direction reasserts,
the AUC collapses.
The final AUC of 0.422 falls below both FedAvg (0.565)
and SCAFFOLD ($\approx$0.500), confirming that server-side
momentum accumulation under extreme class asymmetry
destabilises rather than accelerates convergence.

\subsection{SCAFFOLD Results}
\label{sec:scaffold-results}

SCAFFOLD tells a more interesting story.
It starts identically to FedAvg at round~1
(F1~= 0.429), but by round~2 the control-variate
correction has shifted the decision boundary so far
toward Non-Luminal that the model now predicts
Non-Luminal for every patient (F1~= 0.200, and it
stays there for all 20 rounds).

Two things stand out.
First, the AUC at round~2 reaches 0.601 --- higher
than FedAvg manages after 19~rounds.
This means SCAFFOLD has learned genuinely useful
representations of both classes; the problem is not
the backbone but the classifier head sitting on top
of it.
Second, SCAFFOLD under near-IID ($\alpha=100$) gives
AUC~= 0.370, below random chance.
This confirms that the instability is caused by class
imbalance, not by non-IID data distribution ---
a finding that directly motivates the FedSCRT design.

\subsection{FedSCRT: Federated Classifier Retraining}
\label{sec:fedscrt-results}

FedSCRT, which applies a federated class-balanced classifier
retraining stage after SCAFFOLD backbone convergence, achieves
a macro F1 of \textbf{0.6289} (AUC-ROC = 0.6874,
accuracy = 70.3\%, Table~\ref{tab:fl-results}).

Three things are worth noting.

The F1 of 0.629 closes 84.1\% of the gap left by
FedAvg, reducing the distance to the centralised
baseline from 0.239 to 0.038~F1 points.

The AUC of 0.687 is slightly \emph{higher} than the
centralised baseline of 0.674 --- the federated model
actually discriminates better than the single-fold
centralised model, because three heterogeneous hospital
distributions provide more training diversity than
one fold of the same dataset.

The near-IID comparison results reveal a counterintuitive
finding.
FedAvg under $\alpha=100$ achieves the same macro F1
of 0.4286 as under $\alpha=0.5$, confirming that the
trivial Luminal-prediction collapse is not driven by
heterogeneity alone but by the dataset's 3.2:1 class
imbalance in the training partition.
SCAFFOLD under $\alpha=100$ produces an even more
pathological result: macro F1 = 0.4286 (majority-class
prediction) combined with AUC-ROC = 0.3702, which falls
below the random-chance threshold of 0.5.
An AUC below 0.5 indicates that the model's probability
scores are anti-correlated with the true labels: the
backbone assigns higher Non-Luminal probability to
Luminal patients, and vice versa.
This inversion confirms that SCAFFOLD's instability
under class imbalance is not a consequence of
statistical heterogeneity (it persists even when
$\alpha=100$ eliminates the non-IID component)
but of the class imbalance gradient asymmetry itself.
The control-variate correction amplifies the minority-class
gradient signal even under near-IID conditions,
inverting the representation rather than improving it.
This finding extends the scope of the thesis contribution
beyond a non-IID result: the combination of FedSCRT's
class-balanced head retraining (Section~\ref{sec:fedscrt-theory})
and the decoupled backbone training is beneficial
regardless of the partition heterogeneity degree.

\section{Convergence Analysis}
\label{sec:convergence-results}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Centralised Training Curves}
\label{sec:centralised-convergence}

The Stage~2 validation macro F1 trajectory followed a
consistent pattern across folds.
During epochs~1--54, the LDAM loss drives steady but slow
improvement, with the model progressively learning to
allocate attention to the minority classes.
The activation of ASAM at epoch~55 consistently produces
a step improvement in validation F1, attributed to the
flatter loss basin that ASAM seeks: a flatter minimum
generalises more reliably to the validation fold's
slightly different patient distribution.
The subsequent activation of SWA at epoch~65 further
stabilises the trajectory by averaging checkpoints,
reducing epoch-to-epoch variance in the validation metric.
This sequence is most clearly visible in fold~2, which
is the first fold to complete all 80 Stage~2 epochs
without interruption, achieving Stage~2 peak F1 of $0.336$
before cRT raises it to $0.368$.

For the binary fold~0 experiment, the Stage~2 training
trajectory reaches a peak validation F1 of 0.6037 before
the cRT stage raises the final result to 0.6673,
a gain of 0.064 attributable to the class-balanced
classifier retraining described in
Section~\ref{sec:stage3}.
The binary task benefits more substantially from cRT
than the four-class task, consistent with the observation
that the LDAM-trained backbone produces high-quality
representations (AUC = 0.6874 after FedSCRT) while the
linear head requires explicit rebalancing to translate
those representations into calibrated class predictions.

\subsection{Federated Convergence Curves}
\label{sec:federated-convergence}

to the centralised baseline, with both diagonal entries
non-zero.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:ch4-conclusion}
% ============================================================

This chapter has reported the experimental results of
three interconnected studies.

The centralised four-class pipeline achieves ensemble
macro F1 = 0.342 (95\% CI: [0.301, 0.382]) without
tumour segmentation masks, with the HER2 F1 of 0.20
confirming the fundamental data scarcity ceiling of
46 minority-class training samples.

The centralised binary baseline achieves macro F1 = 0.667
(AUC = 0.674) on the held-out fold~0 validation set,
establishing the performance target for federated
optimisation.

The federated experiment reveals a hierarchy of
algorithmic performance under extreme non-IID conditions
($\alpha=0.5$, Hospital~1 Non-Luminal dominant,
Hospitals~2 and~3 strongly Luminal dominant).
FedAvg collapses to trivial majority-class prediction
(F1 = 0.429, AUC = 0.565), confirming that unweighted
gradient aggregation fails under dominant-class asymmetry.
SCAFFOLD acquires discriminative representations faster
than FedAvg, reaching AUC = 0.601 by round~2 versus
FedAvg's round~19, but overcorrects the decision
boundary due to the extreme imbalance gradient asymmetry
(F1 = 0.200).
FedSCRT resolves the head miscalibration by decoupling
backbone learning from classifier calibration: after
SCAFFOLD convergence, a federated class-balanced retraining
stage yields macro F1 = 0.629 and AUC = 0.687, closing
84.1\% of the federated performance gap and exceeding the
centralised AUC by 0.013 points.

The General Conclusion synthesises these findings,
positions them within the federated medical imaging
literature, and identifies the principal directions
for future work.
