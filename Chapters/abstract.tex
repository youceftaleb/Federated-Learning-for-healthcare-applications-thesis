\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

Breast cancer comes in different biological subtypes,
and knowing which subtype a patient has determines the
right treatment: some subtypes respond well to hormone
therapy, while others need chemotherapy.
Doctors can estimate the subtype from an MRI scan,
and artificial intelligence can help automate this task.

The difficulty is that no single hospital has enough
patient scans to train a reliable AI model on its own,
and privacy laws prevent hospitals from simply sharing
their patient data with each other.

This work explores a technique called federated learning,
which allows several hospitals to train one shared AI
model together without any hospital ever sending its
patient images to the others.
Only the model itself, not the data, moves between
hospitals.

We found that simply applying federated learning does
not work well in this setting, because hospitals tend
to see very different numbers of patients from each
subtype, and some subtypes are naturally much rarer
than others.
Standard federated methods get confused by this
combination and end up making poor predictions.

To address this, we developed a new training procedure,
which we call FedSCRT, that separates two parts of the
learning process: first the hospitals jointly learn to
recognise general patterns in the images, and only
afterwards does each hospital fine-tune the final
decision step using a balancing technique that treats
every subtype fairly, regardless of how rare it is
locally.
This two-step approach recovers most of the performance
lost to the standard federated method, while keeping
all patient data within each hospital at all times.

We also built an educational web application that
demonstrates the complete system in action, showing
how doctors, patients, and researchers could each
interact with such a platform in practice.

\vspace{1em}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Federated learning, breast
MRI, cancer subtype classification, data privacy,
class imbalance, medical imaging.
