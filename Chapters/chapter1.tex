\chapter{Chapter 1 -- Medical Context and AI Foundations for Healthcare Data Analysis}
\section{Introduction}
The deployment of artificial intelligence in healthcare does not begin with model 
architectures or optimization algorithms, it begins with a clear understanding of 
the environment in which those models must operate. Before a federated learning 
system can be designed, trained, or evaluated, three fundamental questions must 
be answered: \textit{where does the data come from and why can it not be freely 
shared?}, \textit{what forms does that data take and what clinical phenomena does 
it encode?}, and \textit{what computational tools are capable of extracting 
meaningful knowledge from it?}

This chapter addresses each of these questions in turn, laying the conceptual and 
technical groundwork for the federated learning system developed in subsequent 
chapters. The first section examines the landscape of healthcare institutions as 
fragmented custodians of sensitive patient data. It introduces the concept of data 
silos, discusses the technical, organizational, and legal barriers to cross-institutional 
data sharing including the GDPR and HIPAA regulatory frameworks and motivates 
the need for collaborative learning paradigms that respect data locality. The second 
section characterizes the medical data modalities at the heart of this thesis.
The nature of these data sources are discussed alongside the challenges 
they pose for integration and analysis. The third section introduces the artificial 
intelligence foundations required to process these modalities, covering the 
spectrum from classical machine learning algorithms to deep learning architectures
including CNNs, RNNs and establishing the evaluation 
framework used throughout this work.

Together, these three pillars define the problem space within which federated learning is positioned 
as both a necessary and principled response.

\section{Healthcare Data and Collaborating Institutions}

Healthcare data is one of the most valuable and sensitive types of information in modern society, as it contains detailed records about patients’ physiological conditions, diagnoses, treatments, and outcomes \cite{olivia2026secure}. These data are generated and collected across a wide range of healthcare institutions, including hospitals, specialized clinics, general practitioner offices, and research centers. Each institution typically maintains its own information systems and databases, resulting in fragmented data storage and limited interoperability across institutions \cite{jensen2012mining}.

\subsection{Healthcare Institutions and Data Silos}

Healthcare institutions differ in scale, specialization, and technological infrastructure. Large hospitals often rely on Electronic Health Records (EHRs), which store structured data such as laboratory results and prescriptions, as well as unstructured data such as clinical notes and medical reports \cite{jensen2012mining}. In contrast, specialised centres rely primarily on high-dimensional imaging data, such as volumetric MRI sequences and physiological signals including electroencephalography (EEG) recordings.

This heterogeneity contributes to the emergence of \textit{data silos}, which refer to isolated repositories of data that are not easily accessible or shareable across systems or institutions. Data silos arise due to differences in database formats, lack of interoperability standards, and institutional policies restricting data access. As a result, the potential of large-scale data analysis and machine learning is significantly limited, since robust models require diverse and representative datasets \cite{Peeyush2025healthcare}.

\subsection{Challenges of Data Sharing in Healthcare}

Sharing healthcare data across institutions is a complex process that involves multiple challenges. First, there are \textbf{technical challenges}, including heterogeneous data formats, inconsistent data quality, and lack of standardized terminologies. For instance, similar clinical variables may be recorded differently across institutions, making integration difficult \cite{kahn2016transparent}.

Second, \textbf{organizational challenges} play a major role. Healthcare institutions may be reluctant to share data due to concerns about data ownership, competitive advantage, or administrative complexity. Establishing agreements for data sharing often requires significant coordination and trust between stakeholders.

Third, \textbf{privacy and security concerns} are critical. Healthcare data contains highly sensitive personal information, and any breach or misuse can have severe consequences for patients. Therefore, strict measures must be taken to ensure data confidentiality, integrity, and secure access \cite{olivia2026secure}.

\subsection{Legal and Ethical Constraints (GDPR, HIPAA)}

The sharing and processing of healthcare data are governed by strict legal and ethical regulations. In the European context, the \textit{General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)} establishes comprehensive rules for the protection of personal data. It requires explicit patient consent, enforces data minimization principles, and mandates anonymization or pseudonymization when data is used for research purposes \cite{EU2016GDPR}.

Similarly, in the United States, the \textit{Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA)} defines standards for safeguarding medical information. HIPAA regulates how healthcare providers and organizations store, access, and transmit patient data, and it imposes strict penalties in case of data breaches \cite{hipaa1996}.
In Algeria, the same obligations apply under Law~18-07, which governs any processing of health data by Algerian institutions regardless of whether the data are transferred abroad.

These regulations highlight the importance of developing methods that enable data utilization while preserving patient privacy.

\subsection{Need for Collaborative Learning in Healthcare}

Given the challenges associated with data sharing, there is a growing need for approaches that allow collaborative analysis without requiring direct data exchange. Traditional centralized machine learning approaches, which rely on aggregating all data into a single repository, are often impractical in healthcare due to regulatory and privacy constraints.

As a result, distributed learning paradigms such as \textbf{Federated learning} have emerged as a promising alternative. These approaches enable multiple institutions to collaboratively train models while keeping the data locally stored. Such strategies allow leveraging large-scale, diverse datasets while preserving data privacy, thereby addressing one of the key limitations of conventional machine learning in healthcare \cite{kairouz2021advances}.



\section{Medical Data Modalities}

Healthcare data is inherently heterogeneous, encompassing multiple modalities that differ in structure, dimensionality, and acquisition processes. These modalities include physiological signals, medical imaging, and structured clinical data, each providing complementary information about patient health. The integration of such diverse data sources is essential for accurate diagnosis, prognosis, and personalized treatment, but it also introduces significant computational and methodological challenges \cite{kus2024medseg}.

\subsection{Medical Imaging}

\subsubsection{Overview of Medical Imaging Modalities}

Medical imaging provides visual representations of internal body structures and is a cornerstone of modern diagnostic medicine. Common imaging modalities include X-ray, Computed Tomography (CT), Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI), and ultrasound, each offering distinct advantages in terms of resolution, contrast, and clinical applicability \cite{kus2024medseg}. These imaging techniques generate high-dimensional data that require specialized processing methods for analysis.

\subsubsection{Cancer Sub-Types and Imaging-Based Diagnosis}

Medical imaging is extensively used in oncology for tumor detection, classification, and monitoring. Different cancer sub-types exhibit distinct visual patterns in imaging data, which can be exploited using machine learning and deep learning models for automated diagnosis. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), in particular, have demonstrated remarkable performance in image-based cancer classification tasks, enabling early detection and improved clinical decision-making \cite{esteva2017dermatologist}. However, variations in imaging protocols and equipment across institutions can introduce significant variability in the data.

\subsection{Clinical and Structured Patient Data}

In addition to signals and images, healthcare systems generate structured data in the form of electronic health records. These include demographic information, laboratory results, medication history, and clinical observations. Such data are typically stored in tabular formats and are essential for predictive modeling tasks such as disease risk assessment and outcome prediction \cite{miotto2017deep}. Despite being more structured, clinical data often suffer from missing values, inconsistencies, and biases, which must be addressed during preprocessing.

\subsection{Challenges of Multimodal Medical Data Integration}

The integration of multiple medical data modalities presents significant challenges. First, modalities differ in terms of scale and representation, ranging from time-series signals (EEG) to high-dimensional images and structured tabular data. Second, aligning these modalities requires synchronization and proper feature extraction techniques. Third, multimodal datasets are often incomplete, as not all patients undergo the same tests or imaging procedures.

From a machine learning perspective, combining multimodal data can improve model performance by capturing complementary information. However, it also increases model complexity and computational cost. These challenges are further amplified in distributed settings, where data are stored across multiple institutions, reinforcing the need for advanced learning paradigms capable of handling multimodal and decentralized data \cite{ngiam2011multimodal}.

\section{Artificial Intelligence in Healthcare}
% ============================================================

\textbf{Artificial intelligence (AI)} denotes the branch of computer science dedicated to
designing computational systems capable of performing tasks that ordinarily require
human cognitive faculties, including logical reasoning, perceptual interpretation,
natural language understanding, and autonomous decision-making under uncertainty
\cite{russell2016artificial}.

\textbf{Machine learning (ML)} is a subfield of AI in which algorithms acquire
predictive or descriptive capability directly from empirical data, without being
programmed with explicit task-specific rules.
A learning system estimates a parameterised model by minimising a loss function
over a labelled or unlabelled training corpus, thereby inducing a mapping from
inputs to outputs that is expected to generalise to previously unseen observations;
depending on the nature of the supervision signal, ML methods are broadly
categorised as supervised, unsupervised, semi-supervised, or reinforcement learning.

\textbf{Deep learning (DL)} is an advanced branch of ML that employs hierarchical
neural network architectures composed of many successive non-linear transformation
layers to learn distributed representations from raw data at progressively
increasing levels of abstraction.
By composing simple parametric functions across many layers, deep models discover
task-relevant features automatically from unprocessed inputs without relying on
hand-engineered feature extractors, a property that has proven transformative for
high-dimensional unstructured modalities such as medical images, biosignals,
and genomic sequences.

In healthcare, AI has shown significant potential to transform disease
diagnosis, prognosis, and patient management by
leveraging large and complex medical datasets including clinical records, medical images,
biosignals, and genomic profiles. The following sections present the
foundational AI concepts that underpin this work, from classical machine learning to deep 
learning architectures, along with the evaluation framework used to assess model performance.

\subsubsection{Definition}

Machine learning (ML) is a subfield of AI in which algorithms automatically learn patterns 
from data to make predictions or decisions without being explicitly programmed for each 
task. ML models are trained on historical data such as patient 
clinical records, laboratory results, or annotated medical images and are then expected 
to generalize to unseen cases. Formally, each observation in the training corpus consists of an input feature vector paired with a known target label; the learning algorithm adjusts its internal parameters so that the mapping from inputs to outputs minimises a prescribed loss function over the training distribution. This inductive process is conditioned on the availability of a sufficiently large and representative labelled corpus: without adequate data, the estimated mapping is unreliable and unlikely to generalise to unseen examples \cite{ibmml}.
In healthcare, ML has been applied to a wide range of 
problems including disease classification, survival prediction, patient stratification, 
and clinical decision support.

\subsubsection{Types of Machine Learning}
% ------------------------------------------------------------
There some variations of how to define the types of Machine Learning
Algorithms but commonly they can be divided into categories according
to their purpose which each having special characteristics and applications.

\paragraph{Supervised Learning}
involves training a model on a labeled dataset, where each input sample 
$\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{X}$ is paired with a known output $y \in \mathcal{Y}$. The model 
learns a mapping function $f: \mathcal{X} \rightarrow \mathcal{Y}$ by minimizing a loss 
function over the training data.
There are two main categories of supervised learning:

\textbf{Regression algorithms} predict output values by identifying linear 
relationships between real or continuous values (e.g., temperature, salary).
Regression algorithms include linear regression, random forest and gradient boosting,
as well as other subtypes.

\textbf{Classification algorithms} predict categorical output variables 
(e.g., “spam” or “not spam”) by labeling pieces of input data. 
Classification algorithms include logistic regression, k-nearest 
neighbors and support vector machines (SVMs), among others \cite{ibmmltypes}.

In healthcare, supervised learning is the most widely 
used paradigm, with applications including tumor classification from imaging data, 
epilepsy detection from EEG signals, and prediction of patient readmission 
rates.

    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figures/supervised_learning.jpg}
    \caption{Supervised learning schema \cite{geeksforgeeks2026mltypes}}
    \label{fig:supervised}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Unsupervised Learning}
deals with unlabeled data, where the algorithm seeks to discover 
hidden structure or groupings within the dataset. Common techniques include clustering 
(e.g., K-means, DBSCAN) and dimensionality reduction (e.g., PCA, autoencoders).
Unlike supervised learning, there
is no predefined correct output; the algorithm’s objective is to autonomously
explore the data to extract meaningful structures. Its
task is to discover hidden patterns, clusters, or relationships directly
from the input data \cite{ibmmltypes}.

In healthcare, unsupervised methods are used to identify patient subgroups,
uncover latent disease phenotypes, and support exploratory data analysis.

\paragraph{Semi-Supervised Learning}
combines a small amount of labeled data with a large pool of 
unlabeled data during training. This paradigm is particularly relevant in medical 
applications, where expert annotation of data such as delineating tumor boundaries 
in MRI scans is expensive, time-consuming, and requires 
specialized clinical knowledge so it aims to leverage the structure of
the unlabeled data to improve the performance of the prediction task,
which would otherwise be limited by the small amount of labeled
data\cite{ibmmltypes}\cite{chapelle2006semi}.

\paragraph{Reinforcement Learning (RL)}
involves an agent that learns optimal decision-making 
policies by interacting with an environment and receiving reward signals.
Reinforcement learning involves learning through trial and error by interacting with
the environment. Each action generates a reward, and the goal is to find the optimal
strategy that maximizes the total reward. The system learns solely from the reinforcement
signal—that is, feedback from its actions. This approach is particularly well-suited for
dynamic environments such as high-frequency trading, where manually defining effective
strategies is difficult \cite{ibmmltypes}.

In healthcare,RL has been explored for sequential treatment planning,
dynamic dosage optimization, 
and personalized therapy recommendation \cite{gottesman2019guidelines}.

\subsubsection{Common Machine Learning Algorithms}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Several ML algorithms have been widely adopted in healthcare classification and prediction 
tasks. The most relevant to this work are briefly described below:

\begin{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Logistic Regression}: supervised machine learning algorithm used 
    for binary classification tasks, where the objective is to model the 
    probability that a given input belongs to a particular class \cite{ibmlogistic}.
    Given an input feature vector $x = (x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n)$, the model 
    computes a linear combination of the inputs:

    \[
    z = w^T x + b = \sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i x_i + b
    \]

    where $w = (w_1, w_2, \dots, w_n)$ represents the weight vector and $b$ is
    the bias term. This linear output is then passed through the sigmoid 
    (logistic) function \ref{fig:sigmoid} to obtain a probability value between 0 and 1:

    \[
    \sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}}
    \]

    Thus, the predicted probability is given by:

    \[
    P(y = 1 \mid x) = \sigma(w^T x + b)
    \]

        The model is trained by minimizing the binary cross-entropy loss function:

    \[
    \mathcal{L}(w, b) = - \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^{m} \left[ y^{(i)} \log(\hat{y}^{(i)}) + (1 - y^{(i)}) \log(1 - \hat{y}^{(i)}) \right]
    \]

    where $m$ is the number of training samples, $y^{(i)}$ is the true label, 
    and $\hat{y}^{(i)}$ is the predicted probability. Optimization is typically
    performed using gradient descent, where the parameters $w$ and $b$ are 
    iteratively updated to minimize the loss. Logistic regression is widely 
    used in medical applications due to its simplicity, interpretability, and
    effectiveness in modeling binary outcomes such as disease presence or 
    absence.

    \begin{figure}[H]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figures/Sigmoid-Activation-Function.png}
        \caption{the Sigmoid function \cite{geeksforgeeks2025sigmoid}}
        \label{fig:sigmoid}
    \end{figure}

    \item \textbf{Decision Trees}: supervised machine learning models used for both 
    classification and regression tasks, which operate by recursively 
    partitioning the input feature space into subsets based on decision 
    rules. Given a dataset, the model selects features and corresponding 
    thresholds to split the data in a way that maximizes class separation. 
    At each node, the quality of a split is evaluated using an impurity 
    measure such as entropy or Gini index \cite{ibmdecision}. The entropy of a node is defined as:

    \[
    H(S) = - \sum_{k=1}^{K} p_k \log_2(p_k)
    \]

    where $p_k$ is the proportion of samples belonging to class $k$ in the 
    subset $S$, and $K$ is the number of classes. Alternatively, the Gini 
    impurity is given by:

    \[
    G(S) = 1 - \sum_{k=1}^{K} p_k^2
    \]

    The objective is to choose the split that minimizes impurity in the 
    resulting subsets. For entropy, this is achieved by maximizing the 
    information gain:

    \[
    IG(S, A) = H(S) - \sum_{v \in Values(A)} \frac{|S_v|}{|S|} H(S_v)
    \]

    where $A$ is the feature used for splitting, $S_v$ is the subset of 
    samples where feature $A$ takes value $v$, and $|S|$ denotes the number 
    of samples. The tree is grown recursively until a stopping criterion is 
    met, such as reaching a maximum depth or having a minimum number of 
    samples per leaf. Decision trees are particularly useful in healthcare
    due to their interpretability, as the resulting model can be visualized 
    as a set of human-readable decision rules for diagnosis and prediction 
    tasks.

    \begin{figure}[H]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figures/DecisionTree.jpg}
        \caption{Decision tree example \cite{ibmdecision}}
        \label{fig:dt}
    \end{figure}

    \item \textbf{Random Forest}: Random forests are ensemble 
    learning methods that improve the performance and robustness of 
    decision trees by combining multiple trees trained on different 
    subsets of the data. Instead of relying on a single tree, a random 
    forest constructs $T$ independent decision trees and aggregates their 
    predictions. Each tree is trained on a bootstrap sample drawn with 
    replacement from the original dataset, a process known as bagging. 
    Additionally, at each split, only a random subset of features is 
    considered, which introduces further diversity among the trees and 
    reduces overfitting.

    For a classification task, each individual tree $h_t(x)$ outputs a 
    class prediction, and the final prediction of the random forest is 
    obtained by majority voting:

    \[
    \hat{y} = \mathrm{mode} \{ h_1(x), h_2(x), \dots, h_T(x) \}
    \]

    For regression tasks, the prediction is typically the average of the 
    outputs:

    \[
    \hat{y} = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} h_t(x)
    \]

    The use of bootstrap sampling and random feature selection ensures 
    that the individual trees are decorrelated, which reduces the variance
    of the overall model without significantly increasing bias. As a 
    result, random forests achieve better generalization performance 
    compared to a single decision tree. In healthcare applications, they 
    are widely used for classification and risk prediction tasks, as they
    can handle high-dimensional data, capture complex feature interactions,
    and provide measures of feature importance that help interpret the 
    contribution of different clinical variables. They also have applications within computational 
    biology, allowing doctors to tackle problems such as gene expression 
    classification, biomarker discovery, and sequence annotation. As a 
    result, doctors can make estimates around drug responses to specific
    medications \cite{ibmrandom}.

    \item \textbf{Support Vector Machines (SVM)}: supervised machine 
    learning algorithms used for classification and regression tasks, 
    which aim to find an optimal decision boundary that maximizes the 
    separation between classes. Given a training dataset 
    $\{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^{m}$ where $x_i \in \mathbb{R}^n$ 
    and $y_i \in \{-1, +1\}$, SVM seeks a hyperplane defined as:

    \[
    w^T x + b = 0
    \]

    where $w$ is the weight vector and $b$ is the bias term. The goal is 
    to find the hyperplane that maximizes the margin, defined as the 
    distance between the hyperplane and the nearest data points from each 
    class, known as support vectors. This leads to the following 
    optimization problem:

    \[
    \min_{w, b} \frac{1}{2} \|w\|^2
    \quad \text{subject to} \quad y_i (w^T x_i + b) \geq 1, \; \forall i
    \]

    This formulation corresponds to the hard-margin SVM, which assumes 
    that the data is linearly separable. In practice, real-world data is 
    often not perfectly separable, and a soft-margin SVM is used by 
    introducing slack variables $\xi_i$ to allow some misclassification:

    \[
    \min_{w, b, \xi} \frac{1}{2} \|w\|^2 + C \sum_{i=1}^{m} \xi_i
    \quad \text{subject to} \quad y_i (w^T x_i + b) \geq 1 - \xi_i, \; \xi_i \geq 0
    \]

    where $C$ is a regularization parameter that controls the trade-off
     between maximizing the margin and minimizing classification errors. 
     For nonlinearly separable data, SVM employs the kernel trick, which 
     maps the input data into a higher-dimensional feature space using a 
     kernel function $K(x_i, x_j)$, allowing linear separation in that 
     space without explicitly computing the transformation. Common kernel 
     functions include the linear, polynomial, and radial basis function 
     (RBF) kernels.

    SVMs are widely used in healthcare applications due to their ability 
    to handle high-dimensional data and their robustness to overfitting, 
    making them suitable for tasks such as medical image classification, 
    disease diagnosis, and bioinformatics analysis \cite{ibmsvm}.

    \item \textbf{k-Nearest Neighbors (k-NN)}: is a non-parametric, 
    instance-based supervised learning method used for both classification
     and regression tasks. Unlike parametric models, KNN does not assume 
     any underlying distribution for the data; instead, it relies on the 
     principle that similar data points tend to be located close to each 
     other in the feature space. Given a query point $x$, the algorithm 
     identifies the $k$ closest samples from the training dataset and 
     assigns a class label based on a majority voting scheme among these 
     neighbors.

    Formally, let $\mathcal{D} = \{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$ be the training dataset. For a new observation $x$, the predicted class $\hat{y}$ is given by:

    \begin{equation}
    \hat{y} = \arg\max_{c \in \mathcal{C}} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{N}_k(x)} \mathbb{I}(y_i = c)
    \end{equation}

    where $\mathcal{N}_k(x)$ denotes the set of the $k$ nearest neighbors 
    of $x$, and $\mathbb{I}(\cdot)$ is the indicator function.
    The notion of “nearness” is defined through a distance metric. The most 
    commonly used one is the Euclidean distance:

    \begin{equation}
    d(x, x_i) = \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{d} (x_j - x_{i,j})^2}
    \end{equation}

    Other distance metrics may also be used depending on the data 
    characteristics, such as the Manhattan distance:

    \begin{equation}
    d(x, x_i) = \sum_{j=1}^{d} |x_j - x_{i,j}|
    \end{equation}

    or the more general Minkowski distance:

    \begin{equation}
    d(x, x_i) = \left( \sum_{j=1}^{d} |x_j - x_{i,j}|^p \right)^{1/p}
    \end{equation}

    The choice of the parameter $k$ plays a critical role in the 
    performance of the model. Small values of $k$ may lead to overfitting 
    and high variance, while large values may smooth the decision boundary 
    and introduce bias. Typically, $k$ is selected using cross-validation.

    One of the main advantages of KNN is its simplicity and ability to 
    adapt to new data without retraining. However, it suffers from high 
    computational complexity during inference, as all training samples must
     be considered. Additionally, KNN is sensitive to the curse of 
     dimensionality, which can degrade its performance in high-dimensional 
     spaces. KNN has also had application within the healthcare industry, 
     making predictions on the risk of heart attacks and prostate cancer. 
     The algorithm works by calculating the most likely gene expressions \cite{ibmknn}.

    \item \textbf{Naive Bayes} is a probabilistic supervised learning 
    algorithm based on Bayes’ theorem, widely used for classification 
    tasks. It is called “naive” because it assumes that all features are 
    conditionally independent given the class label, an assumption that 
    rarely holds in practice but often yields good performance.

    Bayes’ theorem provides the foundation of the model and is expressed as:

    \begin{equation}
    P(C \mid X) = \frac{P(X \mid C)\, P(C)}{P(X)}
    \end{equation}

    where $C$ denotes a class label and $X = (x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n)$ represents
     the feature vector. $P(C \mid X)$ is the posterior probability,
    $P(X \mid C)$ is the likelihood, $P(C)$ is the prior probability 
    of the class, and $P(X)$ is the evidence.
    Under the naive independence assumption, the likelihood can be 
    decomposed as:

    \begin{equation}
    P(X \mid C) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(x_i \mid C)
    \end{equation}

    Thus, the posterior probability becomes:

    \begin{equation}
    P(C \mid X) \propto P(C) \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(x_i \mid C)
    \end{equation}

    The predicted class $\hat{C}$ is obtained by maximizing this posterior:

    \begin{equation}
    \hat{C} = \arg\max_{C} \left[ P(C) \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(x_i \mid C) \right]
    \end{equation}

    In practice, to avoid numerical underflow, computations are often 
    performed in the logarithmic domain:

    \begin{equation}
    \hat{C} = \arg\max_{C} \left[ \log P(C) + \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log P(x_i \mid C) \right]
    \end{equation}

    Different variants of Naive Bayes exist depending on the nature of the
    data. Gaussian Naive Bayes assumes that continuous features follow a 
    normal distribution, while Multinomial and Bernoulli Naive Bayes are
    commonly used for discrete data such as text classification.

    Naive Bayes is computationally efficient and performs well on 
    high-dimensional datasets. However, its main limitation lies in 
    the strong independence assumption, which may reduce its accuracy 
    when features are highly correlated \cite{ibmnaive}.

    \item \textbf{Artificial Neural Networks (ANN)}

    Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) are computational models inspired by 
    the human brain. They consist of layers of interconnected neurons that 
    transform input data into outputs. Each neuron computes a weighted sum 
    of its inputs, adds a bias, and applies an activation function:

    \begin{equation}
    a = \phi\Big(\sum_{i} w_i x_i + b \Big)
    \end{equation}

    where $x_i$ are the inputs, $w_i$ are weights, $b$ is the bias,
    and $\phi$ is an activation function such as sigmoid, tanh, or ReLU. 

    ANNs learn by adjusting the weights and biases to minimize a loss 
    function using optimization techniques like gradient descent. Despite 
    being simple, ANNs can model nonlinear relationships and serve as the 
    foundation for deep learning architectures.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Challenges of Machine Learning in Healthcare}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Despite its promise, deploying ML in healthcare presents several persistent challenges:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Data heterogeneity}: Medical data spans multiple modalities — imaging, 
    signals, text, and tabular records — each requiring distinct preprocessing and 
    representation strategies.
    
    \item \textbf{Class imbalance}: In many clinical tasks, such as rare disease detection 
    or adverse event prediction, the minority class is severely underrepresented, leading 
    to biased models.
    
    \item \textbf{Annotation cost}: Labeling medical data requires expert clinical 
    knowledge, making large-scale annotated datasets difficult and costly to produce.
    
    \item \textbf{Interpretability}: Clinical adoption of ML models requires that 
    decisions be explainable to clinicians, creating tension with the opacity of 
    complex models.
    
    \item \textbf{Privacy and data governance}: Patient data is subject to strict 
    regulatory frameworks (GDPR, HIPAA), severely limiting the ability to centralize 
    data across institutions for training which is a core motivation for federated 
    learning \cite{EU2016GDPR}\cite{hipaa1996}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Deep Learning}
% ============================================================

\subsubsection{Definition}

Deep learning (DL) is a subset of machine learning that uses artificial neural networks 
with multiple successive layers to automatically learn hierarchical representations 
from raw data \cite{lecun2015deep}. Rather than relying on manually engineered features, 
DL models discover task-relevant representations directly from input data, which 
has proven transformative for unstructured modalities such as images, audio, and 
biosignals. ANNs, inspired by the functioning of neurons (\textbf{\ref{fig:neuron}}) in the human brain,
are at the heart of deep learning. Although introduced in the 1940s,
their development was hindered by the lack of data until the era of big
data. Today, they play a key role in the advancement of artificial
intelligence, though they represent just one of many available approaches.

\begin{figure}[H]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figures/biological_neuron.jpg}
        \caption{The biological neuron which inspired ANN \cite{abinaya2025neuron}}
        \label{fig:neuron}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) and Multilayer Perceptrons}

\paragraph{The perceptron}
is the simplest type of artificial neural network (\textbf{\ref{fig:perceptron}}).
It is typically used to produce binary predictions. A perceptron works
only when the data is linearly separable. The inputs of the neuron are
the features x, each multiplied by a learnable parameter. A single neuron
computes:

\[
y = f\!\left(\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i x_i + b\right)
\]

where $w_i$ are learnable weights, $x_i$ are inputs, $b$ is a bias term, and $f$ 
is a non-linear activation function (e.g., ReLU, sigmoid, or softmax). Stacking 
such neurons in layers enables the network to approximate arbitrarily complex functions.

\begin{figure}[H]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figures/perceptron.jpg}
        \caption{The perceptron \cite{geeks2026perceptron}}
        \label{fig:perceptron}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{The multilayer perceptron (MLP)}
is simplest deep learning architecture,it is a fully connected feedforward
network in which every neuron in one layer is connected to every neuron
in the next. An MLP with one hidden layer computes:

\[
\hat{y} = f_2(W_2 \cdot f_1(W_1 \mathbf{x} + b_1) + b_2)
\]

where $f_1$ and $f_2$ are activation functions and $W_1, W_2$ are weight matrices. 
Deeper MLPs can approximate arbitrarily complex functions \cite{hornik1989multilayer}. 
In healthcare, MLPs are commonly applied to structured tabular data such as 
clinical records and laboratory results, and serve as the backbone for more 
specialized architectures such as CNNs and RNNs.

\begin{figure}[H]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figures/DeepNeuralNetwork.jpg}
        \caption{Deep neural network example (MLP) \cite{ibmneuralnetwork}}
        \label{fig:mlp}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Activation Functions}
introduce non-linearity into neural networks, enabling them to model
complex patterns in data. Common activation functions include:

\subparagraph{Sigmoid}
Maps input to a value between 0 and 1:
\begin{equation}
\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-x}}
\end{equation}

\subparagraph{Tanh (Hyperbolic Tangent)}
Maps input to a value between -1 and 1:
\begin{equation}
\tanh(x) = \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{e^x + e^{-x}}
\end{equation}

\subparagraph{ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit)}
Outputs zero for negative inputs and the input itself for positive values:
\begin{equation}
\text{ReLU}(x) = \max(0, x)
\end{equation}

\subparagraph{Leaky ReLU}
A variation of ReLU that allows a small, non-zero gradient for negative inputs:
\begin{equation}
\text{Leaky ReLU}(x) = \begin{cases} x & \text{if } x \ge 0 \\ \alpha x & \text{if } x < 0 \end{cases}
\end{equation}

Activation functions are critical because without them, a neural network
would behave like a linear model, regardless of its depth.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figures/activations.png}
    \caption{Comparison activation functions. Generated using NumPy \cite{numpy} and Matplotlib \cite{matplotlib}.}
    \label{fig:activation_functions}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Training Deep Neural Networks}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Deep learning models are trained by minimizing a \textit{loss function} $\mathcal{L}$, which measures the discrepancy between predicted outputs $\hat{y}$ and ground-truth labels $y$. For multi-class classification tasks, the standard choice is the \textbf{categorical cross-entropy loss}:

\[
\mathcal{L} = -\sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{c=1}^{C} y_{i,c} \log(\hat{y}_{i,c})
\]

where $N$ is the number of training samples, $C$ the number of classes, and $\hat{y}_{i,c}$ the predicted probability for class $c$.

\paragraph{Backpropagation}
Model parameters are optimized using \textbf{backpropagation}, an algorithm that computes the gradient of the loss function with respect to each network parameter using the chain rule. In essence, backpropagation consists of two main phases:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Forward pass:} Input data is passed through the network to compute predictions and the associated loss.
    \item \textbf{Backward pass:} Gradients of the loss with respect to each weight are calculated and propagated backward through the network.
\end{enumerate}
These gradients indicate how each parameter should be adjusted to reduce the loss. 

\paragraph{Optimizers}
While backpropagation computes gradients, an \textbf{optimizer} determines how to update the weights based on these gradients. Popular optimizers include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD):} Updates parameters using gradients computed over mini-batches of data. It introduces noise in the updates, which can help escape local minima.
    \item \textbf{Momentum:} An extension of SGD that accumulates a velocity vector in the direction of past gradients to accelerate convergence, especially in regions with shallow gradients.
    \item \textbf{RMSProp:} Adjusts the learning rate for each parameter individually based on a moving average of recent gradient magnitudes, stabilizing training in non-stationary settings.
    \item \textbf{Adam} \cite{kingma2015adam}: Combines the benefits of momentum and RMSProp, using adaptive learning rates and velocity accumulation, often leading to faster and more stable convergence.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Overfitting and Underfitting}
\textbf{Overfitting} occurs when a model performs well on the \textbf{training} dataset
but poorly on the \textbf{test} data meaning the model is just memorising the training
data, resulting in poor generalization to new samples. Conversely,
\textbf{underfitting} happens when a model is too simple to capture
underlying patterns, yielding poor performance on both training and
test data.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figures/overfitting_underfitting.jpg}
    \caption{Overfitting and underfitting \cite{geeks2025overfitting}.}
    \label{fig:overfitting}
\end{figure}

Strategies to address these issues include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Regularization:} Techniques like L1 (lasso) and L2 (ridge) add a penalty term to the loss function, discouraging overly large weights and promoting simpler models.
    \item \textbf{Dropout:} Temporarily deactivates random neurons during training, forcing the network to learn redundant and more robust representations.
    \begin{figure}[htbp]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figures/dropout.jpg}
        \caption{Dropout Regularization \cite{geeks2025dropout}.}
        \label{fig:dropout}
    \end{figure}
    \item \textbf{Batch Normalization:} Normalizes activations at each layer, reducing internal covariate shift and stabilizing learning, which can improve generalization.
    \item \textbf{Data Augmentation:} Artificially increases the diversity of training data via transformations (e.g., rotations, flips), reducing overfitting by exposing the model to more variations.
    \item \textbf{Early Stopping:} Monitors validation performance and halts training when improvements plateau or degrade, preventing the network from overfitting the training set.
    \item \textbf{Hyperparameter Tuning:} Adjusting learning rates, network depth, number of neurons, or batch sizes can help balance underfitting and overfitting.
\end{itemize}

By carefully combining these methods, a network can achieve both good fit to the training data and strong generalization to unseen samples, which is essential for robust deep learning performance.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

CNNs are specialized architectures designed for grid-structured data, most notably 
images. Their core components are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Convolutional layers}: Apply learnable filters (kernels) across the 
    input to extract local spatial features such as edges, textures, and shapes.
    \item \textbf{Pooling layers}: Downsample feature maps (e.g., max pooling), 
    reducing spatial dimensions while retaining dominant activations.
    \item \textbf{Fully connected layers}: Aggregate the extracted feature maps to 
    produce final class predictions.
\end{itemize}

A convolutional operation for a 2D input $\mathbf{X}$ with kernel $\mathbf{K}$ is:

\[
(X * K)[i,j] = \sum_{m}\sum_{n} X[i+m,\, j+n] \cdot K[m,n]
\]

CNNs have become the standard approach for medical image analysis, with successful 
applications in tumor detection from MRI and CT scans, retinal disease grading 
from fundus images, and histopathology slide classification \cite{litjens2017survey}. 
Architectures such as ResNet, VGG, and EfficientNet, pre-trained on large natural 
image datasets, are routinely fine-tuned on medical imaging tasks through 
transfer learning.

    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figures/architecture-cnn-en.jpeg}
    \caption{CNN architecture \cite{afshinecnn}.}
    \label{fig:cnn}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs)}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

RNNs are designed for sequential and temporal data, where the current output depends 
on both the current input and a hidden state encoding prior context:

\[
h_t = f(W_h h_{t-1} + W_x x_t + b)
\]

where $h_t \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is the hidden state at time step $t$, $x_t$ is the 
current input, $W_h$ and $W_x$ are learnable weight matrices, $b$ is a bias vector, 
and $f(\cdot)$ is a non-linear activation function such as $\tanh$. The output at 
each step can be computed as $\hat{y}_t = g(W_o h_t)$, where $g$ is a task-specific 
output activation.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figures/architecture-rnn-ltr.png}
    \caption{RNN architecture \cite{afshinernn}.}
    \label{fig:rnn}
\end{figure}

Despite their theoretical expressiveness, vanilla RNNs suffer from the 
\textbf{vanishing gradient problem} when trained via Backpropagation Through Time 
(BPTT): gradients diminish exponentially as they are propagated through many time 
steps, making it difficult for the network to capture long-range temporal 
dependencies \cite{bengio1994vanishing}. Two gated variants were introduced to 
overcome this limitation:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)} \cite{hochreiter1997lstm}: Augments 
    the recurrent unit with a dedicated \emph{cell state} $c_t$ and three 
    multiplicative gates (the \emph{input gate} $i_t$, \emph{forget gate} $f_t$, 
    and \emph{output gate} $o_t$) which regulate how much information is written 
    to, retained in, or read from the cell state at each step. This gating mechanism 
    allows LSTMs to selectively preserve relevant information over arbitrarily long 
    sequences, mitigating the vanishing gradient problem.

    \item \textbf{Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU)} \cite{cho2014gru}: Proposes a 
    streamlined gating architecture that merges the cell state and hidden state into 
    a single vector, controlled by two gates, an \emph{update gate} and a 
    \emph{reset gate}. GRUs have fewer parameters than LSTMs and are computationally 
    more efficient, while achieving comparable performance across a wide range of 
    sequential learning tasks.
\end{itemize}

RNNs and their gated variants have been extensively applied to healthcare time-series 
modeling, including mortality prediction from ICU records \cite{lipton2016rnn}, 
disease progression modeling, and physiological signal interpretation. However, their 
inherently sequential computation limits parallelization, a shortcoming that 
Transformer-based architectures later addressed.

\subsection{Model Evaluation}

\subsubsection{Confusion Matrix}

The confusion matrix is the fundamental summary of a classifier's behavior on a 
test set, cross-tabulating predicted labels against true labels. For binary 
classification, it yields four quantities:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{True Positives (TP)}: Correctly predicted positive cases.
    \item \textbf{True Negatives (TN)}: Correctly predicted negative cases.
    \item \textbf{False Positives (FP)}: Negative cases incorrectly predicted as positive.
    \item \textbf{False Negatives (FN)}: Positive cases incorrectly predicted as negative.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figures/Confusion_Matrix.jpg}
    \caption{Confusion matrix \cite{botconfusion}.}
    \label{fig:cm}
\end{figure}

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Classification Metrics}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

From the confusion matrix, the following metrics are derived:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Accuracy}: Overall fraction of correct predictions:
    \[
    \text{Accuracy} = \frac{TP + TN}{TP + TN + FP + FN}
    \]
    Accuracy can be misleading under class imbalance, a frequent occurrence in 
    medical datasets.

    \item \textbf{Precision}: Fraction of positive predictions that are truly positive:
    \[
    \text{Precision} = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}
    \]

    \item \textbf{Recall (Sensitivity)}: Fraction of actual positives correctly 
    identified:
    \[
    \text{Recall} = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}
    \]
    In clinical contexts, high recall is often prioritized to minimize missed diagnoses.

    \item \textbf{F1-Score}: Harmonic mean of precision and recall, providing a 
    balanced measure:
    \[
    F_1 = 2 \times \frac{\text{Precision} \times \text{Recall}}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}}
    \]

    \item \textbf{Specificity}: Fraction of actual negatives correctly identified:
    \[
    \text{Specificity} = \frac{TN}{TN + FP}
    \]
\end{itemize}

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{ROC Curve and AUC}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve plots the True Positive Rate 
(Recall) against the False Positive Rate ($FP / (FP + TN)$) across all 
classification thresholds. The Area Under the Curve (AUC) summarizes this 
in a single scalar: a value of 1.0 indicates perfect discrimination, while 0.5 
corresponds to random chance. AUC is threshold-independent and robust to class 
imbalance, making it a standard evaluation metric in clinical AI research.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Convergence Metrics in Federated Settings}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Beyond standard classification metrics, federated learning introduces additional 
evaluation dimensions. The \textbf{convergence curve} which tracks global model 
accuracy or loss as a function of communication rounds is essential for 
assessing the stability and efficiency of training across distributed clients. 
Metrics such as \textbf{rounds to convergence} and \textbf{communication cost 
per round} are used alongside task performance to compare aggregation strategies.
