\chapter{Chapter 1 -- Medical Context and AI Foundations for Healthcare Data Analysis}
\section{Introduction}
The deployment of artificial intelligence in healthcare does not begin with model
architectures or optimisation algorithms --- it begins with a clear understanding of
the environment in which those models must operate.
Before a federated learning system can be designed, trained, or evaluated, three
fundamental questions must be answered: \textit{where does the data come from and
why can it not be freely shared?}, \textit{what forms does that data take and what
clinical phenomena does it encode?}, and \textit{what computational tools are capable
of extracting meaningful knowledge from it?}

This chapter addresses each question in turn.
Section~\ref{sec:healthcare-data} examines the landscape of healthcare institutions
as fragmented custodians of sensitive patient data, introduces the concept of data
silos, and motivates collaborative learning paradigms that respect data locality.
Section~\ref{sec:medical-modalities} characterises the medical imaging modality
central to this thesis.
Section~\ref{sec:ai-healthcare} introduces the AI foundations required to process
these modalities and establishes the evaluation framework used throughout this work.

\section{Healthcare Data and Collaborating Institutions}
\label{sec:healthcare-data}

Healthcare data is one of the most valuable and sensitive types of information in
modern society, containing detailed records about patients' physiological conditions,
diagnoses, treatments, and outcomes \cite{olivia2026secure}.
These data are generated across a wide range of institutions --- hospitals, specialised
clinics, and research centres --- each maintaining its own information systems and
databases, resulting in fragmented storage and limited interoperability \cite{jensen2012mining}.

\subsection{Healthcare Institutions and Data Silos}

Large hospitals rely on Electronic Health Records (EHRs) storing structured data
(laboratory results, prescriptions) alongside unstructured clinical notes, while
specialised imaging centres depend primarily on high-dimensional volumetric sequences
such as DCE-MRI \cite{jensen2012mining}.
This institutional heterogeneity produces \textit{data silos}: isolated repositories
that are inaccessible across systems due to incompatible database formats, absent
interoperability standards, and policies restricting data access.
As a result, the potential of large-scale machine learning is significantly constrained,
since robust models require diverse and representative training data \cite{Peeyush2025healthcare}.

\subsection{Challenges of Data Sharing in Healthcare}

Sharing healthcare data across institutions involves three categories of challenge.
\textbf{Technical challenges} include heterogeneous data formats, inconsistent data
quality, and absent standardised terminologies that make cross-site integration
difficult \cite{kahn2016transparent}.
\textbf{Organisational challenges} arise from concerns about data ownership and
the administrative complexity of establishing inter-institutional sharing agreements.
\textbf{Privacy and security concerns} are paramount: healthcare data contains
highly sensitive personal information whose breach can have severe consequences
for patients \cite{olivia2026secure}.

\subsection{Legal and Ethical Constraints (GDPR, HIPAA)}

The \textit{General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)} requires explicit patient
consent, enforces data minimisation principles, and mandates anonymisation when
data are used for research purposes \cite{EU2016GDPR}.
The \textit{Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA)} defines
standards for safeguarding medical information and imposes strict penalties for
data breaches \cite{hipaa1996}.
In Algeria, Law~18-07 of June~10,~2018 establishes equivalent protections governing
any processing of health data by Algerian institutions regardless of whether
the data are transferred abroad.
These regulations make the centralisation of multi-institutional patient data
legally and operationally infeasible without substantial overhead.

\subsection{Need for Collaborative Learning in Healthcare}

Traditional centralised machine learning, which aggregates all data into a single
repository, cannot satisfy these regulatory constraints.
Distributed learning paradigms such as \textbf{federated learning} have emerged
as a principled alternative: multiple institutions collaborate to train models while
keeping data locally stored, leveraging large-scale diverse datasets while preserving
patient privacy \cite{kairouz2021advances}.

\section{Medical Data Modalities}
\label{sec:medical-modalities}

Healthcare data encompasses multiple modalities that differ in structure, dimensionality,
and acquisition process \cite{kus2024medseg}.
This thesis focuses on three-dimensional Dynamic Contrast-Enhanced MRI (DCE-MRI);
the dataset, acquisition parameters, and class distribution are described in detail
in Chapter~3 (Section~\ref{sec:dataset-description}).

\subsection{Medical Imaging}

Medical imaging provides visual representations of internal body structures and is
a cornerstone of modern diagnostic medicine.
Common modalities include X-ray, Computed Tomography (CT), Magnetic Resonance
Imaging (MRI), and ultrasound, each offering distinct advantages in resolution,
contrast, and clinical applicability \cite{kus2024medseg}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{figures/xray_mri_ct.jpg}
    \caption{Medical imaging modalities: X-ray, CT, and MRI \cite{perlove2025imaging}.}
    \label{fig:xray_mri_ct}
\end{figure}

Medical imaging is extensively used in oncology for tumour detection, classification,
and monitoring.
CNNs have demonstrated strong performance in image-based cancer classification,
enabling early detection and improved clinical decision-making \cite{esteva2017dermatologist}.
Variations in imaging protocols and equipment across institutions introduce significant
variability that motivates multi-institutional collaborative learning.

\subsection{Clinical and Structured Patient Data}

Healthcare systems also generate structured data in the form of electronic health
records, including demographic information, laboratory results, and medication history,
stored in tabular formats essential for predictive modelling \cite{miotto2017deep}.
Such data frequently contain missing values and biases that must be addressed during
preprocessing.
From a machine learning perspective, combining imaging and structured modalities
can capture complementary information, but increases model complexity --- a challenge
further amplified in distributed settings where data are spread across institutions
\cite{ngiam2011multimodal}.

\section{Artificial Intelligence in Healthcare}
\label{sec:ai-healthcare}
% ============================================================

\textbf{Artificial intelligence (AI)} denotes computational systems capable of
performing tasks that ordinarily require human cognitive faculties
\cite{russell2016artificial}.
\textbf{Machine learning (ML)} is the subfield in which algorithms acquire predictive
capability from empirical data: a parameterised model is estimated by minimising a
loss function over a labelled corpus, inducing a mapping from inputs to outputs
expected to generalise to unseen observations.
\textbf{Deep learning (DL)} extends ML through hierarchical neural network architectures
that learn distributed representations from raw data at progressively increasing levels
of abstraction, discovering task-relevant features automatically without
hand-engineered extractors.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Machine Learning}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Supervised Learning}
involves training a model on a labelled dataset where each input
$\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{X}$ is paired with a known output $y \in \mathcal{Y}$.
The model learns a mapping $f: \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Y}$ by minimising a
prescribed loss function over the training corpus, encompassing both regression and
classification tasks.
In healthcare, supervised methods are applied to tumour classification from imaging
data, disease risk prediction, and patient outcome stratification \cite{ibmmltypes}.

\paragraph{Unsupervised Learning}
deals with unlabelled data, seeking to discover hidden structure without predefined
target outputs through clustering (K-means) and dimensionality reduction (PCA,
autoencoders).
In healthcare, unsupervised methods support patient subgroup discovery and
exploratory data analysis \cite{ibmmltypes}.

Classical machine learning algorithms such as support vector machines, decision
trees, and ensemble methods have been extensively applied in medical imaging tasks
requiring hand-crafted feature extraction;
deep learning supersedes them for volumetric MRI analysis and is the paradigm
adopted in this work.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Deep Learning}
% ============================================================

Deep learning uses artificial neural networks with multiple successive layers to
automatically learn hierarchical representations from raw data \cite{lecun2015deep}.
Non-linear activation functions --- most notably ReLU for hidden layers and GELU
for transformer-style blocks --- are essential components of all deep networks used
in this thesis, enabling the network to model complex patterns beyond those
representable by a linear model.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Training Deep Neural Networks}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Deep learning models are trained by minimising a loss function $\mathcal{L}$
measuring the discrepancy between predicted outputs $\hat{y}$ and ground-truth
labels $y$.
For multi-class classification the standard choice is categorical cross-entropy:
\[
\mathcal{L} = -\sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{c=1}^{C} y_{i,c} \log(\hat{y}_{i,c}),
\]
where $N$ is the number of training samples, $C$ the number of classes, and
$\hat{y}_{i,c}$ the predicted probability for class $c$.

\paragraph{Backpropagation}
Model parameters are optimised using \textbf{backpropagation}, which computes the
gradient of the loss with respect to each parameter via the chain rule.
A forward pass produces predictions and the associated loss; a backward pass
propagates these gradients from output to input layers, yielding the direction and
magnitude of the required weight update at each layer.

\paragraph{Optimisers}
An \textbf{optimiser} translates computed gradients into weight updates.
Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) applies mini-batch gradient estimates; Momentum
extends SGD by accumulating a velocity vector that accelerates convergence in
low-gradient regions.
Adam \cite{kingma2015adam} combines adaptive per-parameter learning rates with
momentum accumulation, offering faster and more stable convergence, and is the
default optimiser for the models developed in this thesis.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

CNNs are specialised architectures designed for grid-structured data such as images.
Convolutional layers apply learnable filters to extract local spatial features;
deeper layers compose these into increasingly abstract representations that encode
semantic content.
CNNs have become the standard approach for medical image analysis, with applications
in tumour detection from MRI and CT scans \cite{litjens2017survey}.
ConvNeXt-Nano, the backbone adopted in this thesis
(Section~\ref{sec:convnext-backbone}), is a modern pure-convolutional architecture
that applies these principles at scale while matching the representational capacity
of Vision Transformers within a compact parameter budget.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Model Evaluation}
% ============================================================

The confusion matrix cross-tabulates predicted labels against true labels, yielding
True Positive (TP), True Negative (TN), False Positive (FP), and False Negative (FN)
counts from which all standard classification metrics are derived; confusion matrices
for all experimental results of this thesis are presented in Chapter~4
(Section~\ref{sec:confusion-matrix}).

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Classification Metrics}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

From the confusion matrix, the following metrics are derived:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Accuracy}: $\frac{TP + TN}{TP + TN + FP + FN}$.
    Accuracy can be misleading under class imbalance, a frequent occurrence in
    medical datasets.

    \item \textbf{Precision}: $\frac{TP}{TP + FP}$.

    \item \textbf{Recall (Sensitivity)}: $\frac{TP}{TP + FN}$.
    In clinical contexts, high recall is prioritised to minimise missed diagnoses.

    \item \textbf{F1-Score}: Harmonic mean of precision and recall:
    $F_1 = 2 \times \frac{\text{Precision} \times \text{Recall}}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}}$.

    \item \textbf{Specificity}: $\frac{TN}{TN + FP}$.
\end{itemize}

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{ROC Curve and AUC}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve plots the True Positive Rate
against the False Positive Rate across all classification thresholds.
The Area Under the Curve (AUC) summarises this in a single scalar: 1.0 indicates
perfect discrimination, 0.5 corresponds to random chance.
AUC is threshold-independent and robust to class imbalance, making it a standard
evaluation metric in clinical AI research.

Federated convergence metrics are defined in Chapter~2,
Section~\ref{sec:convergence}.

\bigskip

While the AI methods described in this section have demonstrated strong performance
in centralised settings, their deployment across real-world healthcare institutions
is fundamentally constrained by data privacy regulations and the impossibility of
pooling sensitive patient data from multiple sites.
These constraints directly motivate the \textbf{federated learning} paradigm
examined in depth in the following chapter.

\section{Conclusion}

This chapter has established the three foundational pillars of this thesis.
The healthcare data landscape is fragmented across institutional silos governed
by GDPR, HIPAA, and Algerian Law~18-07, making the centralisation that conventional
machine learning assumes legally and operationally infeasible.
Medical imaging through DCE-MRI encodes high-dimensional spatial information that
CNNs can exploit for molecular cancer subtype classification.
Deep learning provides the representational power required for these complex
classification tasks, with macro F1 and AUC-ROC as the primary evaluation metrics.
It is the reconciliation of model performance with privacy preservation, data
heterogeneity with collaborative learning, and institutional autonomy with
generalisation that federated learning proposes to achieve --- the subject of
Chapter~2.
